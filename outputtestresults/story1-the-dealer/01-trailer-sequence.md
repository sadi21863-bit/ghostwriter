# THE DEALER

### *A Trailer Sequence*

---

**EXT./INT. ABANDONED FORD ASSEMBLY PLANT — DUSK**

---

**[BEAT 1 — WIDE, LOW ANGLE]**

The doorway is the size of a cathedral mouth. What's left of the roof lets in columns of amber dust-light, falling on collapsed conveyor lines and the bones of cars that never got finished. The DEALER steps through. Full silhouette — long duster, the fanned deck loose at his side like a second hand. He doesn't scan the room. He already knows what's in it. The factory swallows him one slow step at a time.

---

**[BEAT 2 — MEDIUM, EYE-LEVEL]**

His face comes into the light. Weathered. Calm the way a closed door is calm. A single titanium card rises between two fingers — unhurried, the way a man rolls a coin out of habit. His other hand drifts across his body. Passes over the revolver on his hip. *Almost.* Doesn't touch it. The holster is worn dark with years of proximity. The gun inside it hasn't moved.

---

**[BEAT 3 — MEDIUM]**

Forty yards across the factory floor, KESSLER steps out of the shadows and into a hard pillar of light like something that grew in a mill furnace. Iron knuckles already on. He rolls his neck once, slow, and the scar tissue along his jaw catches the light like weld-seam. He smiles — the smile of a man who believes he is owed the outcome.

---

**[BEAT 4 — DYNAMIC]**

The card leaves the Dealer's hand. The air tears. A streak of silver through the dust-columns, edge-on, nearly invisible — then light finds it for one frame, one blinding glint — and it's *gone* into the dark between them. Kessler's smiling mouth is still open.

---

**[BEAT 5 — LOW ANGLE]**

Floor-level. Rusted steel and broken glass and the base of a machine that stamped out chassis forty years ago. Kessler's iron-knuckled hand rests on the ground, fingers loose, pointing at nothing. On his chest — placed, not thrown — the JACK OF SPADES. Titanium. Still.

---

**[BEAT 6 — WIDE]**

The factory door is open. The Dealer walks through it into a sky the color of a spent shell casing — flat grey, no sun left, just the bruised light of a Midwest evening. His coat moves with him. The deck is already back in his hand. He doesn't look back.
