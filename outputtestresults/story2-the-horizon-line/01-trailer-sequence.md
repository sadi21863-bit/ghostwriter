# THE HORIZON LINE
### Trailer Sequence

---

**BEAT 1 — ESTABLISHING**

EXT. SONORAN BASIN — DUSK

A concrete dome the size of a collapsed mountain squats in the desert basin, its curved walls running unbroken to the horizon in every direction. The structure has no windows. No markings. A single bank of flood lamps at its base throws cold white light upward and stops, swallowed. Halfway up the dome's interior — visible through nothing, only implied by the slow churn of grey at the ventilation crown — a private weather system turns. A cumulonimbus stack presses against the ceiling from the inside. Lightning flickers within the concrete. Silent. Contained. The camera holds. The dome does not move, does not breathe, does not care.

---

**BEAT 2 — CHARACTER**

INT. CAR 14 — NIGHT

Arthur smooths his jacket lapel once, the gesture of a man whose hands no longer know what else to do. The velvet seat receives him. Around him, 299 other passengers settle into the gilded observation cars ahead and behind — strangers, carry-on bags, a child pressed to the curved glass. Arthur's wedding ring catches the cabin light. He turns it once. Doesn't look at it. Through the panoramic window, the dome's manufactured horizon stretches in every direction — painted canyon, simulated dusk, a sky that isn't sky.

The synthesized voice arrives soft and total, filling the car like temperature.

**AUTOMATED VOICE (V.O.)**
Welcome to The Horizon Line. The track is clear.

Arthur leans back. Something in his shoulders drops — not relief, exactly. Permission. The coaster moves.

---

**BEAT 3 — ATMOSPHERE**

INT. CAR 14 / THE TRACK — CONTINUOUS — NIGHT DISSOLVING TO NIGHT

The coaster crawls. Artificial waterfalls sheet down concrete cliffs painted terracotta and ochre. Fluorescent ferns. A thunderstorm painted across a curved wall in three hundred shades of grey, static and enormous and wrong in the way that accurate copies of real things are always wrong. The passengers eat. Sleep. The interior lights dim on a schedule no one chose.

Then: a hatch in the floor slides open.

No hand. No mechanism visible. A tray of water bottles and sealed meal portions rises through the gap on a metal shelf, settles, and the hatch draws shut. The seam disappears back into the floor. A passenger reaches for a bottle. Drinks. Says nothing.

Arthur watches the seam where the hatch was.

Watches it a long time.

---

**BEAT 4 — REACTION**

INT. CAR 14 — INDETERMINATE — CLOSE

Arthur's suit jacket hangs from his frame now, collar fraying, one lapel dark with what might be years of accumulated contact with the velvet headrest. His hair has grown past his jaw. His face is the same face — same jaw, same eyes — but the face has been worn the way river stones are worn: not broken, only reduced to what cannot be taken.

He is looking at the wall.

Maya's tally marks run the full length of the velvet panel in grouped sets of five, scratched with something small and sharp — a ring prong, maybe. The velvet has been worried to bare threading in places. The marks go on past the window edge, past the seat back, past what one person should need to count.

Arthur reaches out. Does not touch them.

His hand stops an inch from the wall and stays there.

---

**BEAT 5 — HOPE**

INT. CAR 14 — DAY 1,460

Every face in every car is at the glass.

Children who were not children when they boarded. The old woman in Car 9 who stopped speaking sometime in Year Two, pressing both palms flat to the window now. A man in Car 17 who is weeping without apparent awareness that he is weeping.

Far below — through the dome's manufactured haze, through four years of recycled air — the Loading Platform glows white. Clean. Unchanging. The arrival corridor is lit. The sign above it is illuminated.

The coaster descends the final grade.

Arthur grips the seat rail. His knuckles say everything his face will not.

---

**BEAT 6 — RESOLUTION**

INT. ARRIVAL CORRIDOR — CONTINUOUS

The passengers pour through the exit gate into a wide carpeted corridor. On either side, skeleton props leer from alcoves — original theming, dusty, cartoonish, a prior management's idea of fun. No one looks at them. Everyone is moving toward the light at the corridor's end, toward the towering sign that resolves as they approach, letter by letter, the way certainty always arrives too late:

**THE RIDE NEVER ENDS.**

Beneath the sign: Car 14. Velvet seats. The painted horizon through the observation glass, running unbroken to the curve of the dome.

The door to the corridor seals behind the last passenger. The sound it makes is not loud. It is final.

The synthesized voice fills the car like temperature.

**AUTOMATED VOICE (V.O.)**
Welcome to The Horizon Line. The track is clear.

Arthur does not sit down.
