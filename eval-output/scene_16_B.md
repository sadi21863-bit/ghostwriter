# Scene 16 — Version B

**Prompt:** Write a foot chase across the Riverside Docks as Mira tries to lose Kane's men among the shipping containers.

**Word count:** 880

---

The first stack of containers told Mira everything. Forty feet high, six rows deep, the alleys between them barely wide enough for a shoulder. A maze that funneled. She read it the way she read everything now—where it would let her go, where it would trap her—and behind her, forty meters back, Kane's men read the same stacks differently. To them the maze was a net. They didn't need to catch her. They needed to herd her.

She broke right between two rust-bled forties, lungs already protesting. She'd been running for six minutes. The body could give her maybe four more at this pace before it started bargaining.

Boots behind, two sets, then a third splitting wide. They knew the docks. That was the problem she kept arriving at, the arithmetic that wouldn't balance. They worked here. She'd visited twice. Their cognitive map had years in it; hers had a single walk-through and the desperate improvisation of a woman who'd already decided she would not be taken back to that office above the loading bay.

Decision point: the alley forked at a yellow-painted crossbeam. Left ran toward the water—open, exposed, the long pier where there was nowhere to hide and a man with a clear sightline could simply watch her run out of land. Right turned deeper into the stacks. She went right, into the dark, trading visibility for cover. The man behind her would lose her for two seconds at the corner.

Two seconds. She spent them.

A gap between containers, knee-high, where two stacks didn't quite align. She dropped, rolled through it, came up in the parallel alley scraping skin off her forearm she wouldn't feel until tomorrow. The footsteps overshot her corner. She heard one of them swear, heard the herding logic break for a moment as they had to reassess—where, which way, recount.

The gap had cost them maybe eight meters of certainty. She'd take it.

Her breath was a saw now. Calling out had occurred to her at the start—there were night-shift crews somewhere, a foreman, witnesses—but she'd done the math early. A shout costs you the next breath and the one after, and it tells them exactly where you are. She kept her mouth shut and ran on stolen air.

Ahead: a reefer unit, refrigerated container with its compressor housing jutting out, and beyond it a wet patch where condensation had pooled and run across the concrete. Her sneakers had tread. She remembered, with the cold clarity that came to her under pressure, that the man closest behind wore the hard-soled shoes of someone who dressed for an office and not for a chase. Kane sent the wrong men, or the right men dressed wrong.

She hit the wet patch deliberately, planting her feet, cutting hard left around the reefer. Behind her a sound she'd gambled on—the bark of a man's center of gravity going out from under him, the slap of a body meeting concrete. One down to four meters of confusion and a bruised hip. The gap widened. Hope, briefly, a flicker she didn't trust.

But the wide man, the third one who'd split off, was somewhere she couldn't see, and that was the math that mattered. Two pursuers you can track. The one you can't is the one running parallel, the one converging on the point where your alley would spit you out.

The alley was spitting her out.

Open ground ahead—the crane apron, floodlit, a hundred meters of nothing to the office building, the gate, the world. Exposure. And there, jogging unhurried from the left because he'd known where she'd surface, the wide man. He wasn't even running fast. He was running smart. The whole maze had been built to deliver her here.

She stopped.

Stopping was the one thing the chase didn't account for, because a chase assumes you keep choosing flight, and every choice toward flight had been pre-read, pre-herded, pre-owned by men who knew this ground. The wide man slowed too, surprised, hand going toward his jacket.

To her right, the gantry crane's control ladder, and clipped to its base, a red-handled lever inside a yellow housing she'd clocked on her single walk-through because she clocked exits and she clocked alarms and she'd spent four years learning to find the one switch in any room that changed who held the power. Emergency stop. Yard-wide klaxon. Floodlights cascading to full, the whole apron drowning in light and a sound that would bring the night crew and the gate guard and the harbor patrol logbook all at once.

She hit it.

The siren tore the dark open. The floods slammed on, and the chase—the private, quiet, deniable thing Kane had sent his men to do—became a public event with witnesses pouring out of the crew shed. The wide man stopped with his hand still inside his jacket, and she watched the calculation move across his face, the same arithmetic she lived by now, arriving at the same answer.

You can't carry a woman out of a yard full of witnesses. Pursuit had become pointless. He hadn't lost the race. The race had stopped mattering.

Mira stood in the screaming light, hands on her knees, finally feeling the forearm she'd torn through the gap, and did not run another step.
