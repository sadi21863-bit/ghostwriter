# Scene 13 — Version B

**Prompt:** Write Mira's internal monologue as she admits to herself how far she's willing to go to find Priya.

**Word count:** 819

---

The coffee's gone cold. Drink it anyway. Bitter, that flat metal taste at the back of the tongue, like licking a battery, like the wire she's not supposed to be thinking about. Drink it.

Six days dark. Then the docks. Then nothing.

Okay. Inventory. What you'll do. Be honest for once in your—

Make a list. She always made lists. Priya laughed at the lists. *You and your spreadsheets, Mir.* Stop. Not now. The list.

One. Pull the customs manifests. That's nothing, that's paper, anyone could—

Two. The harbor cameras. Need access. Have access? No. Could get it. Through whom. Through the kid at the port authority who owes nobody anything but who has a wife and the wife has a thing about her status and if you knew somebody at immigration you could—

Stop. Listen to yourself.

That's not nothing. That's a man's wife. That's leverage on a man's wife.

But Priya. Six days. Then the docks.

The room is too quiet. Just the breathing. Hers. In, out, the rasp of it, and nothing else, no one else in the apartment, no one listening, no one to perform the not-doing-it for. That's the test, isn't it. What you do when nobody's there to see you flinch.

Don't flinch.

Where's the line. Name it. You're an analyst, you assess thresholds, you've written threat assessments for men who—

Where is the line. Lying. Fine. Lied for the agency for years. Stealing—data, documents—fine, did that yesterday. Breaking a man's life open to pry a key out of it. Is that the line? Is the wife the line?

It doesn't feel like a line. That's the problem. It feels like a step. They all feel like steps. Each one reasonable from the one before it, that's how you get—

That's how Kane got. Each one a step. The asset disappeared and he took a step and then it was just paperwork and then it was just a confirmation hearing and the girl was just—

The girl was Priya.

Jaw tight. The scar pulls when she does that, the old thread of it through the eyebrow tightening, a small dumb signal from the body, *we've been here before.* The op that doesn't have a name anymore because they redacted it out of existence. She did the thing then too. Told herself it was the only—

Necessity. Always necessity. Her conscience handing her the word like a coat. *It's cold out, you'll need it.* Necessity. The wife is a necessity. The leverage is a necessity. Burning Daniel is a—

There it is.

There it is and she didn't even decide it just now, that's the thing, that's the cold thing dropping through her. She's not weighing it. She already weighed it. Tuesday. When she didn't call him. When she had the manifest and the dock ping and her thumb knew his number and she put the phone face down on the table and finished the coffee and told herself *later, when I know more, when there's something worth—*

That wasn't deferral. That was the decision. Made and dressed up as patience.

So this whole list, this careful honest accounting, *where is the line*—it's theater. Performed for the only audience left, which is her, which is the breathing in the silent room. She isn't deciding how far she'll go. She went. Days ago. She's just now reading the receipt.

Strange. It steadies her.

Should feel worse. Should feel like the floor going. Instead it's the opposite, the way a fever breaks, the way a room stops spinning when you finally name what you drank. She's not the woman trying to find her limit. She's the woman who passed it and kept walking and only now turned around to see the markers behind her, small in the distance, the wife, the kid, Daniel, all of it already chosen.

She knows who she is now. That's all that changed. Not what she'll do—that was settled. Just that she can no longer say she doesn't know.

And the worst part: it makes her faster. The hesitation was never doubt. The hesitation was just not wanting to *know.*

Priya. Six days, then the docks, then send Reyes the message that ends Reyes.

Her hand is over the phone. Just hovering. Glass cold under the fingertips, screen black, her own face in it half-lit. He'll get the message and he'll come and once he comes he's in it, all the way, the wife and the kid and the thing after that and the thing after that, and he won't be able to stand between her and the door anymore because he'll be on her side of it.

Send it.

In a minute.

Let him have the minute. Let him have five. Five more minutes of being a man who doesn't yet know what she's already decided he's going to help her do.

Drink the coffee. It's cold. Drink it anyway.
