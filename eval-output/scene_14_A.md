# Scene 14 — Version A

**Prompt:** Write the scene where Mira realizes Kane's men have found the safehouse and are closing in from both ends of the street.

**Word count:** 1338

---

The dryers came on at seven, all six of them, and for a moment Mira let herself believe the rhythm was a comfort. It wasn't. It was cover. Her ears had been trained on better men than the ones outside, and she'd learned long ago to read a thing's usefulness in both directions: the same noise that hid her movements hid theirs.

She was crouched over the drive when the taillights swept the high window for the third time.

Third. She catalogued it the way she catalogued everything now, without feeling, because feeling came later or it came not at all. A car that passes once is going somewhere. A car that passes twice has missed a turn. A car that passes three times in eleven minutes is establishing a perimeter, and the only question left was how many of them there were and which exits they'd already accounted for.

"Mira." Daniel's voice, low, from the doorway he'd been standing in since he arrived—because he always stood between her and any door, the way some men touched wood for luck. "You're not breathing."

"I'm breathing."

"You're holding it. You do that when the math gets bad."

She didn't answer. She was watching the window, where the sodium glow had settled back into its dirty orange, and she was thinking about the laundromat's two doors. The front, onto the street. The service alley in the back, which fed to a loading bay that fed to—she'd walked it, she walked every safehouse three times before she trusted it—a chain-link gate that someone had been kind enough to repair last week.

Last week. The gate had been broken for two years and last week someone fixed it.

The calculation moved through her like cold water. They hadn't just found her. They'd had time. They'd prepared the ground. A repaired gate was not the work of a team sent tonight to arrest a disgraced analyst; it was the work of people who'd known where she was for days and had been waiting for a reason to be patient about it.

"There's a car at the front," she said. "And the back's already sealed. The gate."

Daniel crossed the room. He didn't crowd her—he'd learned not to—but he looked where she looked, and she watched the tiredness in his eyes sharpen into the thing that had made him good once, before her. "Then they're coming in. We go out the bay, we take whoever's on the gate, we—"

"No." She said it too fast, and heard herself, and made herself slow down. "If they wanted me in custody they'd have come at six, when the laundromat closed and there were no witnesses. They'd have come quiet. They're not coming in, Daniel. They're waiting for dark."

He frowned. "Waiting for what."

"For the dryers to stop. For the last of the foot traffic. For the building to be empty above us." The understanding finished assembling itself and she felt it land in her stomach like something swallowed wrong. "They're not going to arrest me. There's no version of me in a courtroom that helps Kane. There's a version of me in a fire that does."

The dryers thrummed on, indifferent, six steel drums turning in their staggered rhythm, and she thought: that's why three passes and no entry. They're counting the cycle. They know when it ends.

Daniel was quiet for a moment. When he spoke, his voice had gone very flat, which was how she knew he believed her. "Then we leave now. We don't wait to find out if you're right."

And there it was—the place where the math turned ugly, the place she'd known they would reach the second she saw the repaired gate. She looked at the drive in her hand. Half-loaded. The Foundation's payroll routing through three shell donors, the dates, the wire that moved four days before Priya went dark—all of it real, all of it sitting on a machine that needed another nine minutes to finish encrypting and pushing to the dead-drop server. Nine minutes she did not have if she ran. Nine minutes that, if she took them, she would be taking from his life as much as her own.

"I need the upload to finish," she said.

"Mira."

"If we run with the drive and they catch us, it dies with us. If it's on the server, it doesn't matter what they do to me. To us." She corrected it deliberately, made herself say *us*, because she owed him at least the honesty of the pronoun. She had not called him for this. He'd come because she'd told him three weeks ago she would never freeze him out again, and then she had, and then he'd shown up anyway, which was the most damning thing anyone had ever done to her.

"You want to sit in a building they're going to burn," he said slowly, "so a file can finish copying."

"I want Kane to pay for what he did to her." It came out cleaner than she expected, and that was the part that frightened her—how little it cost to say. Somewhere in the last six days the goal had shifted under her without her permission. She'd started this to find Priya. To find her *alive*, the word she no longer let herself finish. And now she was crouched in a flooding basement choosing the upload over the exit, and she understood with a terrible flat clarity that she had stopped believing her sister was at the end of this. What was at the end of this was Kane. Only Kane. She had become a woman who would trade a living man's life for a dead woman's vindication, and she could not find, anywhere in herself, the place where she was supposed to be ashamed of it.

That absence—that was the thing she should have run from. Not the men outside.

Daniel saw it. He always saw it; that was the curse of him. "You already decided," he said. "You decided before you told me any of this."

"Yes."

"And you're telling me now because—" He stopped. He let the question hang, and the not-finishing of it was its own accusation, because they both knew the answer. She was telling him now because she needed a second pair of hands and a body between her and the bay door, and because somewhere under the analyst there was still a person who didn't want to do this alone, and she despised that person tonight for slowing her down.

"You can go," she said. "The bay's still open for maybe another twenty minutes. I'm not—I won't pretend this is fair to you."

He laughed, short and without any humor in it, and didn't move toward the door.

The upload bar crept. Outside, the third car—or a fourth, she'd lost certainty and that loss of certainty was itself information—slowed, and its lights painted the wet wall sulfur-bright, and held there a beat too long before sliding off. She counted the dryers. Two had gone quiet. The rhythm was thinning.

She set the drive down beside the machine, fully loaded now save for the last push, ready to pull and run the instant the bar filled, and she rested two fingers on it the way you rest a hand on something you're not sure you'll be allowed to keep.

A car door slammed in the street. Close. Front of the building, by the sound, by the way it came down through the floor instead of around the walls.

Ninety seconds, she thought. Maybe less, before someone tried the laundromat's front door and found it locked an hour early, and started to wonder why.

She turned to Daniel. She didn't say *run* and she didn't say *stay*. She let him see the question instead, the only one that mattered, the one she had no right to ask and was going to ask anyway:

*How much are you willing to lose, to make sure he doesn't get away with it?*
