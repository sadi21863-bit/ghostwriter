# Scene 09 — Version B

**Prompt:** Write the scene where Mira runs through the Foundation tower as the alarms go off, trying to reach the stairwell before security seals the floor.

**Word count:** 776

---

The badge reader blinked red. Mira already knew the green had been a courtesy — the file copied to the drive in her jacket, the cursor still hanging mid-transfer when the desk phone three offices down rang and didn't stop.

Then the lights changed.

Not off. Worse. The recessed ceiling strips dropped to a low blue, and every glass partition on the forty-first floor went opaque at once — privacy mode, security mode, the building closing its eyes. The hum she'd stopped hearing an hour ago resolved into a single tone, somewhere behind her teeth.

She moved.

The corridor ran the spine of the floor, glass on both sides, and glass was the problem — no cover, no corners, just a long bright box and the elevator bank at the far end she could not use. Stairwell. North side. Past the open atrium where the floor cut away into a four-story void and the railing was the only thing between her and the lobby below.

Forty meters. She counted them in steps, not seconds.

The first door she passed sealed as she passed it, a magnetic clunk that traveled up through the soles of her feet. The building was locking back to front. She was running ahead of a wave.

Twenty-five meters. The atrium opened on her right, cold air rising out of the void, and her own reflection ran beside her in the dark glass, splintering at each pane-seam, a woman in pieces.

A guard came up the spiral from the atrium's lower level. Not fast — he didn't know yet which threat the alarm meant, only that there was one — and that was her half-second. She didn't slow. She put the long planter between them, a low concrete trough of ornamental grass, and went around its blind side while he cleared the top of the stair. His flashlight swept the corridor and washed the glass white. For one beat she saw nothing. Tunnel of light. She ran into it blind, trusting the count.

Eighteen.

Her heel caught the planter's lip. The concrete took skin off her shin and turned her, hard, into the railing — and the railing was waist-high and the void was right there, the lobby floor a long pale square below, and her hands found the cold steel and held. She did not go over. She hung folded across it, breath gone, the whole shipwreck of her own weight trying to continue past the rail.

"Stop." The guard, behind. Closer than the count said.

She pushed off the rail. The shin screamed now, late, the pain arriving after the fall the way it always did. She ran on it anyway. Limping wrecruits her left leg, shortens the stride, and the count she'd built was wrong now, every step costing more than the last.

Ten meters. The stairwell door. Steel, no glass, a green exit sign above it gone dim in the blue light. And a second guard already at it, already reaching for the panel that would seal it from the inside.

His hand was on the keypad. Cold building, cold fingers — he fumbled the code, one digit, two. She saw the small failure of his hands and ran at it.

Five meters.

He got the third digit. She heard the lock motor wake. The door was sliding its bolt home, the kind of motion that does not stop once it starts, and she was not going to reach it, and the count said no, and her legs said no, and the shin said worse than no.

She threw the drive.

Not at him — past him, low and hard down the corridor behind the door's swing, and his eyes went after it because that was the thing he'd been told to protect and he could not help it. His hand left the panel. The bolt was three-quarters home. She hit the door with her shoulder and her good leg drove from the floor and the steel ground against the half-seated bolt and shrieked and gave eight inches.

Eight inches. She went sideways through it. The frame took her hip, the bad shin folded, and she was in the stairwell with cold concrete under her cheek and the door slamming its full weight against the bolt it could no longer pass.

The drive was on the wrong side. With the guard.

She got her hands under her. Below, the stairwell dropped forty floors in a square spiral, sodium-yellow, smelling of wet concrete and cigarettes. Above, faint, the thunder of boots on the next landing up.

She chose down. She started running before she could feel her own legs answer.
