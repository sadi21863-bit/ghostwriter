# Scene 10 — Version B

**Prompt:** Write the scene where Mira is trapped in the flooded sub-basement of her safehouse and realizes she isn't alone down there.

**Word count:** 1125

---

The laundromat machines ran all night, and Mira had learned to love the sound. From the basement it came through the floor as a low industrial heartbeat, steady, anonymous, the kind of noise that swallowed a person whole. She moved through the back room past the lint traps and the bleach drums, lifted the maintenance grate, and went down the iron rungs into the dark she had measured a hundred times. Eleven steps. Cold air rising. The smell of mildew and gun oil from the wall locker where she kept what she could not keep anywhere else.

She was thinking about Priya. She was always thinking about Priya now, the way you keep touching a tooth that's gone soft. The hard drive was in the sub-basement, in a sealed lockbox bolted to the foundation, behind a panel she'd cut herself eight months ago. On it: forty minutes of a meeting Kane swore never happened. She had until dawn to be gone with it and on a train.

Her boot came down and found water.

Not the inch of seep that was always there in the spring. Her foot went in to the ankle, then the calf, cold closing around the muscle like a hand. She stood very still on the second-to-last rung. The fast part of her had already decided something the slow part hadn't caught up to yet, and her heart was going before she knew why.

A pipe. The copper main behind the south wall. She could smell it now under the mildew, a bright mineral sharpness, and she could hear it — not the drip she knew but a steady seam of water under pressure, somewhere below the surface, filling the room from the bottom up.

She dropped in.

The water took her to mid-thigh and kept the cold of stone. Her flashlight beam cut a wedge through it and found the far wall, the panel, the bolt heads winking under a skin of moving water. The lockbox was below the line now. She would have to go under.

She wedged the flashlight into the elbow joint of an overhead pipe so the beam lay flat across the surface, took three breaths to load her blood, and went down.

The water bit her open eyes. Chlorine and rust, a sting that brought instant tears the water washed away before they formed. She found the panel by feel, found the keyway, worked the pick. Cold fingers. They wouldn't do what she told them — the tumblers were right there, she could feel the first one give and then her hand would shudder and lose it. She came up gasping, went down again. Up. Down. The water was at her hips when she stood, then climbing toward her waist, and she understood with a flat clarity that the exit rungs would be under in twenty minutes and that she was not going to make the lock in twenty minutes.

That was when the breathing started.

She came up from the fourth dive and heard it before her own breath settled — another breath, out of phase with hers, coming off the concrete in a way that doubled it, so that the small room seemed to inhale from two throats. She went still. Held her own air in her chest until it ached. The other breathing went on. Wet. Ragged at the top of each pull, the way a person breathes when they are trying very hard to do it quietly and failing.

It was behind her, in the deep corner the flashlight didn't reach, where the water was darkest and the ceiling came down low.

Her hand went to the small of her back, to the weapon, and her fingers were too cold to feel the grip and she made herself feel it anyway. She did not turn the flashlight. The thing about the dark was that it cut both ways. She moved one step sideways, soundless, putting the pipe and its blade of light between her and the corner, and she said nothing, because the silence was the only thing she still owned down here.

The water lapped against something in the corner. A shape resolved out of the black as her eyes gave up on color — a head, shoulders above the surface, motionless, the breath coming and coming.

"Mira."

Her whole body unclenched and re-clenched in the same second, fear converting straight to fury with no station in between.

"You've been gone an hour." Daniel's voice was wrong with cold, the words shaking apart at the edges. He came forward into the light and she saw he was in to his chest, that he'd come down the way she had and gone the wrong direction looking for her in the dark, and that he'd found the deep end. "Laundromat owner let me in. You didn't answer. I came down and the water was already—" He stopped. His teeth were going. "I couldn't find the ladder again."

"You followed me here."

"You stopped telling me things." He said it without heat, which was worse. "So I stopped waiting to be told."

The water reached her ribs. Each of his breaths slapped back off the walls and into her ears, and under it she could hear the seam of the pipe still feeding the room, patient as Kane himself.

"There's a lockbox," she said. "Behind the panel. Under the water now. I have to open it before we go."

He looked at her for a long moment, and she watched him do the math she'd been refusing to do — the rate of the water, the depth of the rungs, the time it would take to pick a lock blind and submerged with two people now drawing down the same thinning air at the top of the room.

"Leave it," he said.

"I can't."

"Mira. Leave it. We go now, we both make the ladder."

"It's her," Mira said. "It's the only thing on this earth that says what he did to her. I leave it, I've got nothing, and he buries her twice."

She saw it land. She saw him understand, finally and completely, that there was no number she would not trade — that he was standing in rising water in the dark and she was telling him, gently, that the box mattered as much as the man, and that he had walked down here into the place where she had stopped being someone he could pull back.

"Hold the light," she said, and pressed the flashlight into his hands before he could answer, and took the last full breath of the high air, and went under into the cold and the copper toward the lock that had not yet given.
