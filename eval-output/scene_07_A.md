# Scene 07 — Version A

**Prompt:** Write the scene where Mira waits in the dark of her safehouse, certain Kane's car is about to pull up outside.

**Word count:** 1402

---

The car was still half a block away when she stopped breathing.

She had the window cracked two inches, the way she always left it — enough to hear, not enough to be a silhouette. The basement sat below grade, so the glass gave her the street at ankle height: a slice of wet asphalt, the base of the lamp post, the sodium glow that turned everything the color of old rust. Headlights washed across the puddle near the storm drain. Slow. Too slow for someone who knew where they were going.

She had told herself for three days that she wasn't waiting for this. She had cleaned the Sig twice today anyway.

The car came up the block at the pace of a man reading addresses. Dark sedan, German maybe, the kind of quiet engine that cost more than the building above her. Kane drove himself when it mattered. That was the thing people got wrong about him — they pictured the motorcade, the aides, the armor of attention. But the senator she'd studied for two years liked to do the important things with his own hands. It made him feel clean.

She thumbed the safety off.

The condensation on the glass blurred the world into smears. She wiped a streak with two fingers and the cold beaded back almost at once, the air down here thick enough to taste, mildew and gun oil and the river that never fully drained out of the floor. She could see the front quarter of the car now. She could not see the plate. She could not see the driver. She could see that it had slowed, and slowing was the only fact that mattered, and slowing meant nothing — meant a wrong house, meant a phone held to a face, meant a hundred ordinary things that ordinary cars did on ordinary streets.

She thumbed the safety back on.

This was the part she'd lost the agency over, if she was honest, which she tried to be only at moments like this when there was no one to perform for. Not the leak. The waiting. The way she would hold a frame too long, certain, certain, building the case in her head until the case became the world and the world had to obey it. Daniel used to put his hand flat on the table between them when she did it. *You're solving it before it happens,* he'd say. *You don't know yet.* And she would tell him she did. And sometimes she was right, which was worse, because being right taught her the wrong lesson.

The car stopped.

Not in front of her. Two doors down, by the laundromat, where the only entrance to this place was — through the dryers in the back, past the woman who took cash and asked nothing. If it was Kane, he wouldn't know that. If it was Kane, he wouldn't need to. He'd send people who knew everything and let her exhaust herself guessing which fact had been the one that gave her up.

She had her boots on. She'd had her boots on for three days.

Off again. On the table, the Sig caught the orange light along its slide. She could go out the service tunnel behind the boiler, the one she'd checked the first night and twice since, water to the ankle the whole way, and surface in the alley behind the noodle place, and be three neighborhoods gone before anyone made the door. Or she could stay. Stay and let him come down the stairs into the only room where she knew the angles better than he did. Run was sensible. Run was what you taught analysts to do. Stay was what you wanted to do when the man who'd disappeared your sister was finally close enough to put your hands on.

Want and sensible had stopped being the same thing the day Priya's number went dark.

She picked up the Sig. She did not thumb the safety. She watched the slice of street and the stopped car and waited for the door to open, for the dome light, for the shape of a man who'd spent two years certain he was the only adult in any room, and she let herself imagine it down to the sound his shoes would make on the wet steps —

The car pulled away.

It rolled forward, gathered itself, turned at the corner without signaling, and was gone. The street went back to being a street. The lamp hummed. Water moved somewhere under the floor with the long patience of water. She stood with the gun in her hand and her own pulse loud in her ears and the specific, hollowed-out feeling of having braced against a wall that wasn't there. She'd built the case again. The case had been wrong again. Somewhere Daniel's hand was flat on a table he wasn't sitting at.

She exhaled. Set the Sig down. Her hand was steady, which she noted with the small contempt she reserved for her own body's competence.

Her phone buzzed against the table.

She looked at it the way you look at a sound in a house you thought was empty. Unknown number. No name, no text, just the gray bubble loading a photograph that built itself top to bottom across the screen — rust first, the corrugated wall of a shipping container, the kind stacked four-high at the Riverside yard. Then the seal stenciled across the steel. She knew the seal. She'd watched it printed on letterhead and lobbies and the side of trucks that moved at hours trucks didn't usually move. The Aldric Foundation. Open hands, the logo a designer had been paid to make mean *transparency.*

And against the rust, against the seal, held up by an arm that ended just outside the frame — a jacket. Olive canvas, frayed at the left cuff because Priya chewed her sleeves when she read, a habit Mira had given her along with the jacket itself, fourteen birthdays ago, when olive canvas was the most grown-up thing a nineteen-year-old could imagine owning.

Someone was holding it. Someone wanted her to see it being held.

She understood three things in the space it took the screen to finish loading, and they arrived in order, the way bad things always did when you'd trained for them.

He wasn't coming. That was the first thing. The car wasn't his and the next car wouldn't be either, because he didn't need to come — burying her was a thing he could do later, at leisure, after the hearing, the way you dealt with a problem that had stopped being urgent. She was not urgent. That was almost an insult.

The second thing was the jacket, in someone's hand, at the docks, where Priya's phone had pinged six days into the dark and then never again. The jacket was a fact. The jacket said *here.* Whether it said *alive* she made herself not decide, because deciding was the wall she kept building and she could not afford to be wrong about this one.

The third thing was the worst, and it sat in her chest like the river under the floor. This was not surveillance. Surveillance you didn't get told about. This was an address. He'd taken her sister, and now he was handing her the directions, and a man only handed you directions when the room at the end of them was a room he'd already arranged.

She was being hunted. Or she was being herded. Looking at the photo, at her own gift held up in a stranger's fist against Kane's rusted seal, she could not tell which, and she understood that he'd designed it that way — that the not-knowing was the point, that he wanted her moving before she could think, the way she always moved, certain and early and alone.

She should call Daniel. She had told him she would, the last time, with his hand flat on the table. She had told him and then she had not, and the not was sitting between them now like a third thing in every room.

Her thumb hovered over the screen. The container. The seal. The frayed cuff.

She put her boots — already on — flat on the floor, and reached for the Sig, and did not yet know whose name she'd say first.
