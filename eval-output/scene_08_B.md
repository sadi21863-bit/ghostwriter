# Scene 08 — Version B

**Prompt:** Write the scene where Mira finally learns what actually happened to Priya the night she disappeared.

**Word count:** 1019

---

The recording started playing before Mira's thumb left the screen.

She'd braced for a voice — Priya's voice, maybe, or Kane's. What came first was static, then water. The slap of it against pilings. Then her sister breathing, too close to the mic, the way Priya always held a phone when she was scared and pretending she wasn't.

Mira's hand went flat against her sternum. She didn't decide to do it. The hand was already there, pressing, as if it could hold something in place, and only after did she register the pressure under it — a tightening that ran up into her throat and stopped there, lodged, refusing to move in either direction.

*Just listen,* she told herself. *Listen like an analyst.*

Her jaw set. She knew the muscle that did it, had read the literature once in some forgotten training module — the masseter, clenching, the chin pulling up and tight. She let it. Better that than the other thing happening at the corners of her mouth, the downward pull she could feel building and was fighting with everything she had.

On the recording, footsteps. Priya's whisper: *"—at the third warehouse, the one Kane's people use for—"* A door. The words cut.

Then a man's voice. Calm. Unhurried.

The cold came up Mira's arms in a single sheet, all the hair standing, her skin pebbling beneath her sleeves. She knew that voice. She'd shaken that hand at a fundraiser two years ago and thought, *what a tired old man.*

Daniel said her name. She heard it the way you hear something underwater — shape without edges. He was standing by the door, where he always stood, his broad shape going blurred at the margins because her eyes had stopped working on anything but the phone.

Kane's voice, on the recording, said: *"You should have come to me first."*

Priya's voice, sharper than Mira had given her credit for, even then, even there: *"I did. You buried it."*

A pause. Water. Breathing.

*"Priya."* Almost gentle. *"There's no version of this where you walk out and the program survives. And the program has to survive."*

The recording went to static.

Mira's lungs had stopped. Not metaphor — they had simply quit, a held breath that wasn't a decision, the diaphragm locked, and when her body finally took the air it took it wrong, a hitching pull that caught halfway and stuttered. Her shoulders came up toward her ears. Her spine curled forward over the phone like she was sheltering it from rain.

"Mira." Daniel, closer now. The floor under her shifted — no, that was her own weight, going.

She heard herself make a sound. It wasn't a word. Whatever language she'd been about to use had drained out of her, and what was left was thinner than speech, a single broken exhale through the teeth, the kind of noise the body makes when the mind hasn't caught up to what the lungs already know.

Her fingers wouldn't unclench from the phone. She looked at them — far away, someone else's — the knuckles white, the tendons standing up like cords. *Let go,* she thought, and they didn't.

Daniel's hand closed over her shoulder. She flinched so hard the phone nearly went out of her grip, and then she was just shaking, fine and fast, a tremor in the hands and the chin and the long muscles of her thighs, her body running a current with nowhere to send it. Fight or flight, with nothing to fight, nowhere to run. Six days dark. The phone pinging near the docks. The third warehouse. She'd done the math a hundred times and held the answer at arm's length and now the answer was in her hand, playing, and there was no arm long enough.

"Sit down," Daniel said. "Mira. Sit—"

She sat because her legs folded, not because she chose it. The cold floor came up to meet her. The shaking didn't stop; it changed register, settling deeper, into the chest, a slow heaving that wasn't crying yet — crying was a thing that organized itself and this hadn't organized, this was just the body's machinery seizing and releasing, seizing and releasing, the breath coming in those broken stairs.

Her eyes were dry. That was the part that frightened her, distantly, the analyst in her noting it from very far off: no tears, just the throat clamped shut and the chest heaving against the clamp. Too deep for water. The water comes later, she knew. The water comes when the body decides it's safe enough to fall apart, and her body had not decided that. Her body had decided that the man on the recording was still out there, calm and silver-haired, and that there was work to do.

She pressed the phone to her sternum with both hands now. Bowed over it. Daniel crouched in front of her, not touching, his tired eyes level with hers, his hands open at his sides.

"I'm here," he said.

She got one word out. It cost her everything, scraped raw out of the closed throat, barely sound.

"He—"

And then nothing. The rest of the sentence dissolved. Her jaw worked and made no language. She shook her head, hard, fast, as if she could shake the words loose, and they wouldn't come, because there were no words for the shape of it, only the body holding the phone, only the cold draining slowly out of her arms now, leaving them heavy, leaving them aching the way a limb aches after the blood comes back.

The shaking eased by degrees. Not because it was over. Because the body could only hold that pitch so long before it had to drop, and what it dropped into was worse — a stillness, a flatness, her breathing leveling into something mechanical. She stared at the floor between Daniel's boots. Somewhere underneath the flatness, deep, where she couldn't reach it yet, something was beginning to harden.

Her thumb moved. It found the screen. It dragged the slider back to the start.

"Don't," Daniel said.

She played it again.
