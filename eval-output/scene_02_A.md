# Scene 02 — Version A

**Prompt:** Write the scene where Mira breaks into the Foundation's archive room looking for the shell-company list.

**Word count:** 943

---

The archive room was on the fourteenth floor, behind a door that looked like a wall.

Mira had spent three days learning that. Three days of Foundation floor plans pulled from a building permit database in 2019, cross-referenced against the electrical schematics she'd lifted from a contractor who'd done the retrofit and hadn't thought to wipe his truck's Bluetooth. The door used a capacitive reader disguised as a section of the paneling. No handle. No frame gap. Just a seam you'd only find if you were already looking.

She found the seam at 2:14 a.m.

The building had gone quiet an hour ago — the cleaning crew cycled out at one, the overnight guard made his rounds on a twenty-two-minute loop that she'd timed from the lobby camera feeds she'd been ghosting for a week. She had nine minutes. Maybe ten if he stopped at the water fountain on twelve the way he sometimes did on Tuesdays.

She pressed the bypass chip to the panel. A repurposed transit card reader, recoded with a sequence Daniel had refused to know about. He'd set it on the kitchen table and walked into the other room and she'd picked it up without saying anything. That was the arrangement now.

The seam clicked. The panel swung inward on pneumatic hinges, silent and slow, the way expensive things opened.

Inside: cold air, climate-controlled to sixty-two degrees. The smell of acid-free paper and the faint chemical bite of server cooling fluid. The room was smaller than she'd expected — maybe twenty feet by twelve — lined floor to ceiling with physical document bays and a single terminal mounted on a standing desk in the center. No chairs. Nobody was meant to spend time here.

Mira moved to the terminal first.

The screen was dark but not locked — whoever had been here last had put it to sleep without logging out, which meant she was either lucky or being baited. She took one breath and decided it was luck. Bait would have had the guard loop shortened.

She pulled the drive from her jacket pocket and seated it in the USB port without looking down.

The directory loaded while she crossed to the document bays. The shell-company list might not be digitized — Kane was careful in ways that looked like paranoia until you understood he was just old enough to remember what happened to men who kept records in places that could be subpoenaed. Physical files in a climate-controlled room behind a disguised door in a building he owned outright were not evidence. They were interior design.

The bays were organized by year, then by a letter-number system she didn't recognize. She started with the current year and worked backward. Her fingers moved along the tab edges without pulling — read, skip, read, skip — muscle memory from a job she'd been stripped of, but the muscle hadn't gotten the memo.

She found the 2021 bay.

Behind the first divider: foundation disbursements, public-facing, nothing. Behind the second: correspondence with the oversight committee, redacted copies she'd already seen. Behind the third: a folder with no label, thicker than the others, the cardstock cover worn soft at the corners from handling.

She opened it.

The first page was a transfer authorization. The receiving entity was listed as Calloway Meridian Holdings — a name she didn't recognize, which meant it was good, which meant it was what she was looking for. The authorization was dated eighteen months ago. The signatory line had Kane's signature and below it, a second name she had to read twice.

She read it twice.

The terminal chirped behind her — the drive completing its pull — and she photographed the page twice with her phone before she'd consciously decided to, the motion already happening while some part of her mind was still processing the second name on the signature line.

Deputy Director Carl Wren.

Wren, who had signed her termination paperwork. Wren, who had sat across from her in the review board and said, with the particular sympathy of someone who has already decided, *the evidence is unfortunately clear.* Wren, who had kept his hands flat on the table the entire time, the way people did when they were being careful not to give anything away.

She closed the folder. Replaced it behind the third divider with the same degree of compression it had been filed with — she knew how, she'd been taught how, she'd taught other people how — and crossed back to the terminal to pull the drive.

Eight seconds of margin left on the guard's loop.

She closed the archive panel behind her and stood in the fourteenth-floor corridor, the drive in her pocket and the folder's contents behind her eyes, and did not move until she heard the elevator open one floor down and the guard's footsteps go the other direction.

Then she moved toward the stairs.

The second signature didn't change what she'd come here to do. She understood that. She even believed it, for the length of time it took her to descend four floors in the dark.

By the tenth floor she wasn't sure anymore what she was going to do when she found out how deep Wren went. By the eighth, she was thinking about Daniel in the laundromat two miles east, waiting with the car running and his eyes on the door, and she thought about what his face would do when she told him.

She thought about that for a long time.

She came out through the loading dock and into the sodium-yellow dark, and the thing she couldn't stop thinking about wasn't Kane anymore.
