# Scene 12 — Version A

**Prompt:** Write a charged, vulnerable moment between Mira and Daniel in the safehouse after a near-miss with Kane's people.

**Word count:** 1242

---

Her hands gave her away before her voice did.

The whiskey hit the rim of the glass and skidded, a thin amber line down the side onto the laminate countertop, because her fingers would not hold still. She poured anyway. The pour was the point — the ritual of it, the steadiness she was performing for the man behind her — and the bottle clinked against the glass twice, three times, a small percussive confession.

"You told Vasquez we were across the river." Daniel's voice came flat from somewhere near the window. "We're not across the river."

"He doesn't need to know where we are."

"He's our contact."

"He's a contact." She lifted the glass. Drank. The shaking didn't touch her voice, and she'd learned long ago that this was the only part anyone graded you on. "There's a difference, and the difference is what keeps us breathing."

The industrial heater chose that moment to cycle on, a low mechanical exhale that pushed warm air across the room though it wasn't cold — wrongly timed, the thermostat broken or set by someone who'd never been here in winter. The smell of it came with the heat. Scorched dust. The radiator equivalent of a held breath let out too late.

Daniel didn't move from the window for a moment. Then he did, and his shadow crossed her face on the way — he'd put himself between her and the glass, and behind that, between her and the dark street four floors down. He did this. He had always done this. Stood between her and the door, the window, the line of fire, as if his body were a thing he kept ready for exactly this purpose and nothing else. She used to find it suffocating. Tonight she catalogued it like inventory.

"Look at me and tell me you're thinking straight," he said.

She looked at him. That was easy. Looking at people was the easiest lie in the world.

"Tell me this is about finding her," he went on, "and not about putting Kane's head on the floor. Because those two things stopped being the same thing about a week ago and you didn't tell me when."

"They were never different."

"They were. You just liked them better blurred." He crouched a little, getting down to where she'd half-turned away, refusing to chase her eyes but refusing to let them off either. "I watched you lie to a man who's risked his life for us, and you didn't even slow down. You didn't blink. That's not the leak they pinned on you, Mira. That's not somebody framing you. That was you, choosing."

"I'm keeping us alive."

"You're keeping us *useful.* To you."

The word landed somewhere she hadn't braced. She set the glass down because her hand had started again and she didn't want him to hear it — and the bottom of the glass rattled against the counter, three quick taps, and he heard it anyway. She watched him hear it. Watched his eyes drop to her hand and come back up changed.

"When's the last time you slept," he said, quieter now, the interrogation going soft at the edges and that was worse, the softness was always worse.

"Her phone pinged the docks six days dark." She hadn't meant to say it like that. "Six days, Daniel. You know the curve. You've seen the curve. I've run the curve in my head every hour since and I can't —" The sentence ran out of road. She tried to find the part of herself that finished sentences and it wasn't where she'd left it. "I don't know if she's there. I don't know if she's anywhere. I don't know if I'm walking us toward my sister or toward a place where they already put her in the water, and I keep deciding it doesn't matter which because either way the man stays standing, and that — that's not —"

She stopped. Because it had cracked. The controlled thing, the analyst thing, the surface she ironed flat every morning before the rest of the world saw her — it had split right down the middle and the person underneath had looked out for one second, raw and twenty-nine years deep in a grief she would not name.

And she saw him see it. She saw it reach him, the way pain reaches a man who's loved you wrong and long.

And then — this was the part she would carry, the part she would not be able to un-know about herself — she let it stay on her face one beat longer than the feeling lasted. She felt the feeling recede and she held the expression after it, like keeping a door open after the person had already gone through. Because she needed him. Because a Daniel who'd just glimpsed the wound would not leave a woman alone with it. She knew his shape. She used it.

"Don't," he said.

"Don't what."

"Don't do the thing you're doing right now." His jaw worked. "You think I can't tell the difference between you bleeding and you showing me you're bleeding? I've known you nine years." He straightened up. The shadow came off her face. The window was behind him again, the cold street, the heater still pushing its wrong warmth between them. "That's the worst part. I still can't make myself walk out while there's a chance the first one's real."

The heater cut off. The silence after it was enormous.

"Forty-eight hours," he said. "That's what you've got from me. I'll run the docks, I'll watch your back, I'll be the body in the doorway, all of it. Forty-eight." He picked his coat up off the chair, slow, like it cost him. "But I'm done believing you when you tell me you've got it handled. I'll do the work. I won't take the words anymore."

She should have felt the win. She'd kept him. That was the goal she'd carried into this room and she'd carried it back out intact. Instead something behind her sternum had gone cold and stayed cold, and she understood that she had bought the next two days with the only currency she had left, and that he knew the exact denomination she'd paid in.

He stopped at the doorway to the other room. Didn't turn around all the way. Just enough that she got his profile, the tired eyes, the line of a man doing arithmetic he hated.

"You're going to make me choose," he said. "Between you and what's right. You know that. You've known it for days." A breath. "I just want it on the record that I saw it coming and stayed anyway. So when it happens you can't tell yourself I didn't have a chance to go."

He walked into the dark of the second room and didn't wait for what she might say.

She didn't follow.

She sat with the unanswered question and the unsteady glass and the dead heater ticking as it cooled, and she could not have told anyone — not Daniel, not Priya if Priya were alive to ask, not the version of herself she used to be before the agency taught her what she was good at — whether the not-following was kindness or just the next move.

She poured another inch. Her hand had gone still. She noticed that, and it frightened her more than the shaking had.
