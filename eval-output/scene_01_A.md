# Scene 01 — Version A

**Prompt:** Continue the story: Mira reads Daniel's message about the docks and has to decide whether to go alone.

**Word count:** 1022

---

The message came in at 2:47 a.m.

Mira had been awake since midnight, which was not unusual. Sleep was a negotiation she rarely won. She was sitting on the floor with her back against the radiator — broken, cold, but the right height — studying the printout she'd taped to the wall opposite the mattress. Priya's last six months in photographs, call logs, a map with three locations circled in red marker. The docks were one of them.

Daniel's message was four words.

*Ping confirmed. South pier. Come.*

She read it twice. Then she set the phone face-down on the concrete.

The problem with Daniel was not that he was untrustworthy. The problem was the opposite. He was the kind of person whose word you could build something on, which made him dangerous to her in ways a liar never would be. She knew what he wanted her to do. He wanted her to wait, to come in through the laundromat entrance and sit across from him at the card table and let him lay out a plan with contingencies and fallbacks, a thing with clean lines and an exit strategy. He wanted two of them to go to the docks when it was light enough to see.

The phone said 2:51.

She looked at the photograph she'd pinned at the center of the wall. It was the last one she had of Priya, taken at a conference she hadn't attended herself. Priya in a navy blazer, mid-sentence, the way she always was in photographs — the camera catching her not at rest but in motion, as though stillness were something that happened to other people. She looked sharp. She looked like she knew something.

She'd known something.

Mira got up. She pulled on the gray jacket with the broken zipper on the left pocket and left the right one alone, because she knew what was in it and she needed to know without looking. She checked the time again. The tide at the south pier would be coming in by five. If anything had been left there, anything that floated or could be moved by water, it would not be there by five.

She wrote nothing back to Daniel.

---

The garment district at three in the morning was not empty exactly, but the people who moved through it moved like they didn't want to be counted. Mira moved the same way. She kept her hands out of her pockets, which was a discipline she'd learned from someone who was now probably watching someone else make the same mistakes she had. The street was orange under sodium lights. A truck idled outside a loading dock with its lights off, the driver asleep or performing it. She did not look at him long enough to determine which.

The docks were twenty minutes on foot. She did not take a cab.

She was fourteen minutes out when her phone buzzed.

*Don't.*

Just the one word. Which meant he knew. Which meant he'd been awake too, watching the same information she was watching, arriving at the same conclusion, and had picked up his phone to say the thing he knew she'd already decided against.

She put the phone in her left pocket, the one with the broken zipper.

---

The south pier smelled like brine and rust and something underneath both, something organic and unhelpful to think about. The fog had settled low over the water, and the sodium lights along the dock road made it luminous, the kind of light that flattened depth and made distances wrong. She moved along the outer edge of the shipping containers, keeping the corrugated steel at her left shoulder.

She had been here before. Not this pier, but the geography of it — the way abandoned infrastructure held its breath. She'd spent six weeks in a place like this once, in a life she was no longer authorized to have. She knew what to listen for. She listened.

Water against hull. Somewhere distant, a gull, though it was too dark for gulls. The rhythmic knock of something loose against something fixed.

She found it at the third mooring post.

Not Priya. She would not let herself reach for that before she'd confirmed the evidence around it. What she found was a waterproof case, the kind used for field equipment, the kind she recognized because she had used this brand specifically, had requested it specifically, in a procurement form that had been filed under a project name she was not supposed to know had been closed. It was chained to the mooring post with a cable lock, the combination kind. She crouched over it and studied it without touching it.

The case was dry on top. It had been placed here since the last rain, which meant within the last nine hours.

Her phone buzzed again.

She didn't look at it.

The combination lock was four digits. She tried the only sequence she allowed herself to try first, the one that wasn't hers. She'd been looking at Priya's files for three weeks. She knew Priya's habits, the way you learned someone's habits when they weren't there to have them anymore. She knew Priya had used their mother's birthday as a PIN since she was sixteen and that she had never changed it, despite being told, despite knowing better, despite being the kind of person who was sharper than people gave her credit for.

The lock opened.

Mira did not move for a moment. The fog pressed around her.

Inside the case, packed in foam that had been cut to fit, were three things: a hard drive in a sealed evidence bag, a burner phone with the battery separate, and a folded piece of paper that had been written on in handwriting she recognized the way you recognize a voice before you understand what it's saying.

She unfolded the paper.

The handwriting was Priya's.

Two lines. No greeting, no signature — Priya had always trusted that context would do the work.

*It's not about the program. It's about what he used it to find.*

*Don't come alone next time.*
