# Scene 14 — Version B

**Prompt:** Write the scene where Mira realizes Kane's men have found the safehouse and are closing in from both ends of the street.

**Word count:** 879

---

The phone pinged once and went dark again. Priya's number, the old burner, the one Mira had told her sister to throw in the river two months ago.

She didn't move toward it. That was the first thing the agency taught you and the last thing it let you forget: when the impossible arrives, you sit still and let it cost you nothing. So she sat on the edge of the cot in the flooded basement, water seeping cold through the soles of her boots, and looked at the screen face-up on the milk crate, and she catalogued what she knew.

Priya's burner was supposed to be at the bottom of the river. Either it wasn't, which meant Priya had lied to her, which meant Priya had been making her own moves the whole time. Or someone else had it. Someone who knew that a ping from that number would freeze Mira in place exactly long enough.

She was already reaching for the Sig when the laundromat bell rang upstairs.

One customer. Nine forty at night, in a neighborhood where the machines hadn't worked since spring. Daniel had a key to the side door and Daniel didn't use the bell. She heard the dryer door — the broken one, the one that screamed on its hinge — open and not close. Someone propping a sightline. Someone who'd been told the layout.

That was the detail that rearranged everything. *Told the layout.* The safehouse wasn't in any file. She'd cleared it herself, paid cash, run the laundromat owner three ways before she trusted the man's silence. Which meant the leak wasn't the leak she'd been chasing. It was nearer than that. It was inside the small circle of people who knew where she slept, and that circle had four names in it, and one of them was missing and one of them was dead-drop loyal and one of them was her.

She killed the lamp. Moved to the high window, the one at ankle height to the street, and put her eye to the gap in the cardboard.

Two cars. One at each end of the block, lights off, engines warm enough to fog the cold air at the exhaust. They hadn't come to talk. You didn't bracket a street to serve a warrant; you bracketed a street so that whatever happened in the middle had no witnesses and no exits. This was Kane's grammar — the docks grammar, the off-record grammar — applied to a residential street four miles from the river. He'd brought the docks to her.

And that told her the last thing, the thing she'd been refusing.

If Kane was willing to do this now, *before* the hearing, with cameras two streets over and a confirmation vote that depended on his clean hands — then he wasn't protecting his career. A man protecting his career waits. A man protecting his career lets her career stay buried and her body stay breathing. The disproportion she'd felt for weeks finally found its shape: whatever Priya had touched at the docks, it was large enough that Kane would rather risk the hearing than let Mira reach it. The surveillance program was the floor. There was a basement under it.

Her phone buzzed. Daniel. *Don't go out the laundromat. They're staging in the dry cleaner across — I'm on the roof. I count six.*

Six. For one disgraced analyst in a basement. She typed back: *Did you tell anyone this address.*

The reply took four seconds too long. *No.*

She looked at those four seconds and hated what she could do with them. Daniel had been on the roof, in position, before she'd known she was surrounded. Which meant Daniel had known to be on the roof. Which meant either he'd followed the same men she had, the way a good partner does, the way the man who stood between her and every door he could find would — or he'd been given the time and place by the people setting it. Both were true to him. That was the unbearable thing about Daniel: every loyal explanation and every betraying one fit the same man without a seam.

She had thirty seconds, maybe, before they decided the bell-ring recon was done. She could go up through the coal chute into the alley and trust the man on the roof. Or she could not trust him, and take the storm drain, and leave him up there counting six men with no idea she was already gone — leave him to be the one they found, the one they questioned, the one who'd taken the fall for her once before and could take it again.

The drain was the smart play. The drain was also the first time in her life she'd be doing to Daniel what the agency had done to her: handing a clean man the bill for someone else's debt because it cost her less.

*That's how you become Kane,* she thought. Not all at once. One reasonable basement at a time.

Footsteps crossed the ceiling above her, unhurried. Patient. The way Kane moved when he already knew how a thing ended.

She picked up Priya's pinging phone, put it in her pocket, and went to the coal chute.
