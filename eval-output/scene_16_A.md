# Scene 16 — Version A

**Prompt:** Write a foot chase across the Riverside Docks as Mira tries to lose Kane's men among the shipping containers.

**Word count:** 1750

---

The fog was leaving her.

Mira pressed her shoulder blades flat against the cold corrugated steel of the container and counted the lights as they came on—not all at once, but in a sweep, banks of sodium vapor stuttering awake from the eastern berths inward, each one cutting another bar of orange through the mist. Two minutes ago the yard had been a single gray smear and she had been a ghost in it. Now the fog was thinning to ribbons and the ribbons were dissolving and she was becoming a woman in a dark jacket standing in a grid she did not own.

She had the courier's drive in her inner pocket. She could feel it against her ribs like a second heartbeat, which was sentimental, which she hated, which she noted and set aside.

Three of them. She'd counted three when the lights came up: tactical gear, the unhurried spread of men who'd done this before. They weren't running. That was the thing that turned her stomach. People who ran were afraid of losing you. People who walked had already decided where you'd have to go.

She read the yard the way she'd been trained to, the way she still couldn't stop doing even after the agency had stripped the clearance off her like skin. Stacks ran in long avenues, north to south, four and five containers high. The avenues were chokepoints—predictable, lit, a man at each end and you were a fish in a barrel. The cross-gaps between stacks were the alternatives, narrow seams where a container hadn't aligned with its neighbor, dark and tight and slow. Slow was the cost. Slow bought concealment and spent time.

To the far perimeter was maybe four hundred meters. Between her and it: the operatives, methodically peeling her sightlines away.

She moved.

First seam, left—a body-width gap between two rust-bled walls, salt-rot thick enough to taste, her shoulders scraping both sides. She came out the far end into the next avenue and the calculation hit her in the half-second of exposure: a man at the south mouth, forty meters, head turning toward her. She didn't sprint. Sprinting drew the eye. She stepped back into shadow and let the avenue swallow the line between them, and her pulse climbed anyway because she'd just spent that exit. One option closed. The funnel narrowing by one.

Behind her, somewhere among the stacks, a container shifted on its moorings with a shriek of metal that went up her spine like a finger. The sound was a gift. It covered her footsteps. It was also a reminder that this whole yard was a thing that moved, that tipped, that could be made to crush.

She filed it.

She went up.

There was a ladder welded to the seaward face of the third stack, and she took it fast, hand over hand, adrenaline turning her body into something that didn't feel the cold steel or the strain in her forearms—that would come later, all of it, the way it always came back after, the trembling and the nausea and the bone-deep cost of having borrowed against herself. Not now. Now she got to the top of the second tier and lay flat against the ribbed roof and breathed through her mouth.

From up here she could see them. The map clarified. Two were working the avenues in a slow scissor, sweeping toward the water, herding. The third had broken off east. She understood it instantly and felt the dread bloom clean and cold: they weren't trying to catch her in the stacks. They were closing the yard against the river. They wanted her at the waterline, where the only direction left was down into black water and there was no down from down.

She had perhaps ninety seconds before the scissor closed.

She could call Daniel. The thought arrived fully formed, with his number already there because it had always been there, and she killed it the way she killed everything inconvenient. Calling meant breath she didn't have and meant a thing she'd promised him and then hadn't done, meant pulling him over a line she'd drawn specifically to keep him on the safe side of it. She'd told him to stay out. She'd meant it as protection. She knew, in the cold accounting part of her that never slept, that she'd also meant it as control—that needing no one was the only armor she trusted, that this was exactly the reflex that had cost her everything once already.

She didn't call.

She moved along the roofline instead, low, fast, jumping the gap between two containers—a meter and a half of nothing over a four-story drop, and her foot landed clean and her breath punched out and she kept going. The far perimeter was closer now. Two hundred meters. She could see the chain fence and the gap in it and the road beyond, empty, no car, because she hadn't prepositioned one, because she'd come in believing she'd come out the way she'd come, and that arrogance was now a number subtracting itself from her chances.

Below her, an operative crossed the avenue she'd just abandoned. He looked up.

The light hit her. Orange and total. No more fog to hide in.

"There," he said—not a shout, a radio voice, flat, and that flatness was worse than any shout because it meant procedure, it meant the other two were already adjusting, the scissor reorienting toward her position with the patience of men who knew the yard ended in water.

She went off the stack the only way left: a controlled drop to a lower tier, then a hang, then a fall the last six feet that drove up through her ankles and would hurt enormously in an hour. She hit the ground running now—no point in stealth, stealth was spent—and the avenue opened ahead of her toward the perimeter and she ran flat out, lungs tearing, the drive thudding against her ribs.

A man stepped into the avenue's mouth ahead. Calm. Squared. Cutting the lane.

She broke right into a seam without slowing, slammed her shoulder against steel, felt skin tear and didn't feel it, came out into a cross-passage and a decision: left toward the perimeter through open ground and a man's line of sight, or right into another avenue, longer, hidden, wrong direction. Left was the goal and the gun. Right was alive and away from where she needed to be.

The wrong choice wouldn't kill her. It would just close the yard tighter. She knew that. She chose left anyway, because the goal was the whole point, because she had not run this far and burned this much to die safely in the wrong direction—

—and rounded the corner of the last stack into the open, into the full sodium glare, exposed, two hundred meters of fence and freedom dead ahead, and there was a forklift idling in the gap.

Engine running. Headlights off. A broad shape in the cab she knew before her brain finished the shape, the set of the shoulders, the way he was already half out the door with one hand reaching.

Daniel.

The relief and the horror arrived in the same instant and canceled into something that closed her throat. Because he was here. Because *here* meant he'd found the docks, which meant he'd followed the thread she hadn't shared, which meant Kane's people had now seen them both, two faces instead of one, two names going up the chain in that flat radio voice. Because his being here was the line she'd told him not to cross, crossed, for her, and the cost of it was already being counted somewhere in a building made of glass.

"Get in," he said. Not a question. Not an *I told you so.* Just the door open and his tired eyes on her and behind those eyes the thing he wasn't saying, which was *you didn't call.*

She got in.

The forklift lurched, slow and stupid and miraculous, grinding toward the gap. Behind them the three operatives spilled into the open avenue and stopped—because a forklift at ten miles an hour was not worth a body across a fence and an open inquiry, because Kane's men dealt in things that didn't go on record and a chase into a public street was a record. They stood in the orange light and let the fog take them back, receding, professional, in no hurry, because men who didn't hurry already knew where you'd have to go.

Mira pressed the drive against her ribs and breathed and felt the cost of all of it beginning to arrive—the ankles, the shoulder, the shaking. She had the footage. The footage was real and in her pocket and that meant Kane's confirmation hearing was no longer a thing to be quietly avoided. It was a clock now. It had started ticking the moment a courier's drive left a Foundation pocket and entered hers, and it would not stop.

"You crossed it," she said. Her voice came out wrong, frayed.

"You'd have drowned in the wrong direction." He didn't look at her. His jaw worked. "You always go for the goal. Even when the goal's a gun." A beat. "You said you'd bring me in."

She didn't answer that, because the answer was *I know,* and *I know* was not enough, and she'd spent her whole adult life believing she could carry the weight alone right up until the weight had been her sister and her sister was gone and alone had gotten her exactly here—running across a poisoned dock with a man she'd lied to by omission, both of them now burned, because she could not bring herself to need anyone.

The realization settled into her like the cold finally finding her bones. She could not do this alone. Not anymore. Not because she lacked the will—she had nothing but will—but because alone was the thing Kane was counting on. Alone was how Priya had vanished. Alone was the strategy of every patient man who'd ever decided he was the only adult in the room.

Her phone buzzed.

She looked at it. Daniel glanced over.

Unknown number. Five words on the screen, glowing in the dark cab over the diesel rattle and the slap of river water against hulls neither of them could see.

*I have Priya. Let's talk.*

The dock lights cut past them in orange bars, one after another, all the way to the dark.
