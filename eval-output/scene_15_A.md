# Scene 15 — Version A

**Prompt:** Write the scene where Mira interrogates a captured Foundation operative about Priya's location.

**Word count:** 786

---

The man's name was Ostrowski, and he had been awake for forty minutes before Mira said anything to him.

She sat across the steel table in the gutted office above the marina, a manila folder closed under her folded hands, and she let him look at her. Let him take in the scar through the eyebrow, the stillness. He had the build of someone who'd done this from the other side of the table. That was useful. People who knew the procedure thought they understood the game.

"You stopped for gas," she said finally, "on the night of the eleventh. Off the Bayfront exit. Receipt time, 11:47."

He didn't move. Good control. But his thumb pressed flat against his forefinger, a small ironing motion, and she filed it away.

"I'm not asking you about the gas," Mira said. "I already have the gas."

Silence then, the deliberate kind. She opened the folder a few inches, looked at nothing inside it, and closed it again. He watched her hands the whole time.

"You're with the Bureau," he said. "Or you were."

"Help me understand something." She leaned back. "From where you sit. You drive out past the Bayfront with a passenger you weren't briefed on. You're not a kidnapper, Ostrowski, I've read your file, you do logistics. Transport. So somebody hands you a problem you didn't sign up for and tells you it's nothing. A favor. And now I'm here, and your people aren't, and you're starting to do the arithmetic on what nothing actually cost."

"I didn't have a passenger." Too fast. The denial arrived before she'd accused him of anything specific.

She let that sit. Counted it.

"I didn't say where the passenger came from," she said quietly. "I said you had one."

His jaw worked. He recalibrated — she watched him do it, watched the story reassemble behind his eyes. "There was a transfer. A package. I don't open the packages. That's the whole point of me."

"A package." She nodded slowly, like he'd given her something reasonable. "Cargo. You handle cargo. So this cargo, you dropped it where the manifest told you to drop it, and you drove home, and you slept fine." She paused. "Then why'd you stop for gas at 11:47 when your tank reader pulled from the rental shows you filled at the depot at nine?"

The thumb stopped moving. That was the tell — not motion, but the absence of it. He'd frozen the hand to keep it from speaking for him.

"You stopped," she went on, unhurried, Kane's own tempo though she didn't know it, "because somebody in the back needed something. Air. Water. Quiet. You stopped because cargo doesn't ask for things, and a person does."

"I want a lawyer."

"You're not arrested. There's no lawyer here. There's me, and the question of whether the cargo was breathing when you handed it off." She leaned in, and now her voice dropped all its patience at once, and she hated how easy it came. "Because if she was, you're a witness. If she wasn't, you're the last man who touched her. So you tell me which one you'd rather be, and you do it before I decide for you."

His breathing changed. Shallower, higher in the chest. The prepared man was gone and the cornered one had arrived.

"She was talking," he blurted. "When I dropped her. She was talking, alright? Saying her sister would—" He stopped, and the room got very loud with the thing he'd nearly said.

Mira did not move. She did not breathe. Saying her sister would.

Priya had said *her sister*. Priya had used her, used Mira, as the threat — had believed, in the back of a transport van at midnight, that Mira was coming. That was either the most hopeful thing Mira had ever heard or the cruelest, and she couldn't yet tell which.

"Where," she said. One word. Everything she had behind it kept off her face.

"Pier 9. The cold storage. That's all I know, that's where the handoff—" He shook his head. "But that was the eleventh. I don't know what they did after. I swear to God I don't know after."

The phone ping had been Pier 9. Six days of it dark, then one ping, then nothing. He'd just confirmed a location she already half had — and given her the one thing she hadn't.

Mira stood. Picked up the folder that held nothing.

"You said *talking,*" she said, at the door. "Present tense. You keep saying it like she still is."

He looked up at her, and for one second they both believed it, and that was worse than anything the folder could have held.
