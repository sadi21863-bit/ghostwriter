# Scene 03 — Version B

**Prompt:** Write a quiet conversation between Mira and Daniel where he admits he's been talking to Kane's office behind her back.

**Word count:** 1275

---

The kitchen light had been on for three hours. Mira knew because she'd been watching the time the way she watched everything lately — compulsively, without quite meaning to.

Daniel stood at the counter with his back to her, doing nothing in particular to the coffee. The mug had been full for ten minutes. She'd noticed that too.

"You want to sit down," she said. Not a question.

"I'm fine."

She waited. The refrigerator cycled on, hummed, cycled off. She'd learned patience from a woman who'd taught surveillance methodology for seventeen years, and the lesson had survived everything else the agency had stripped away. Daniel's shoulder blades shifted incrementally under his jacket. Barely perceptible. She perceived it.

"Something happened," she said.

His exhale was slow and controlled and gave itself away precisely because it was so controlled. He turned around. The overhead light was unkind to the specific kind of tired that had accumulated around his eyes — not the tiredness of a late night but the kind that had been layering itself for days. AU4: the brow drawn down and in, held there. Not anger. Sustained engagement with something he couldn't put down.

"I need to tell you something."

Her hands were already cold. The knowledge arrived in her palms before her brain had assembled the words to understand it.

"So tell me," she said.

He pulled out the chair across from her and sat in it slowly, like someone negotiating with a bad knee, though his knee was fine. He set both hands flat on the table between them.

"Kane's office contacted me. About two weeks ago."

The kitchen didn't move. She didn't move.

"His chief of staff. Ames. I don't know how he got my number." He said this last part quickly, and the quickness was its own confession — he'd rehearsed the words in the right order and had still misordered them. "I should have told you immediately. I know that."

"You should have." Her voice came out even. She noted distantly that this was happening — the evenness, the controlled rate — and understood it as a warning sign, the body sealing itself.

"He wanted to know how close you were." Daniel's hands pressed flat against the table, like he was steadying something. "To the docks. To Priya."

"What did you tell him."

"Nothing useful. I gave him—" He stopped. Started again. "I gave him enough that he'd believe I was a real source without—"

"What did you give him."

He looked at her directly for the first time. His chin was down, the slight AU17 raise that turned the jaw into something that had to be held deliberately still. "That you were still running down the phone ping. That you didn't have anything confirmed."

The cold had moved from her hands up through her forearms. She was aware of her own breathing in a way that meant it had changed.

"That was two weeks ago," she said.

"Yes."

"Priya's been dark six days."

"I know."

"You understand what those two numbers mean next to each other."

He looked at the table. "I wasn't — I didn't think about it that way when—"

"Don't." The word was flat and clipped and left no space. She watched his jaw tighten around whatever he'd been going to say. "Don't explain what you were thinking at the time. Tell me what you know right now."

The refrigerator started again. Outside, a car moved down the street and the headlights slid across the kitchen wall and disappeared.

"Ames called again this week," Daniel said. "He pushed harder. Wanted to know if you had physical evidence. Documents. I didn't answer."

"But you picked up."

He didn't say anything. Which was its own answer.

She stood up. She wasn't conscious of deciding to stand — the body had simply made the calculation before she had, some old threat-response that moved her away from chairs and tables and positions of static vulnerability. She went to the window and looked at the street for something that wasn't there.

"Why." She wasn't asking why he'd done it. She already knew why — she could construct the entire architecture of his reasoning from the material she had. She was asking the smaller question. The one with teeth.

"Because you don't tell me anything." His voice changed when he said it. Something released in his chest — she could hear it from across the room, the way the breath support shifted, some door that had been leaned against for a long time. "You haven't for two months. You show me fragments. You give me what you want me to have. And I thought if I—" He stopped. His voice had picked up speed and then caught itself. "They approached me. That part's true. And I thought if I had an open line into his office, I'd know when you were in real danger before you decided to tell me."

"So you gave Kane a line into me."

"No. I gave Kane a line into what I chose to tell them. There's a difference."

"Is there."

The question landed between them like something physical. She turned around. He was looking at her with an expression that required all four of the muscles around his eyes — not performed, not selected, just there. She had a sudden precise awareness that she was looking at someone who was genuinely afraid of her and had been for some time.

"You don't trust me," she said.

"I trust you completely." He said it without hesitation, and it was the complete absence of hesitation that made the distinction clear. "I don't trust what you're willing to lose to do this."

Silence.

Her jaw ached. She hadn't known she'd been clenching it.

"He knows I'm near the docks," she said. "That's what you handed him."

"I didn't know about the ping when I—"

"But you know now." She could hear her own voice — the rate had slowed down, the pitch had dropped slightly, and she recognized both as signals not of calm but of something on the other side of it. "And Ames called again this week. Which means Kane's office is working the clock on something. Which means everything I thought I had two weeks to do, I probably have—" She checked her watch without fully registering what it said. "I don't know. Less."

"I'm sorry."

"I know." She believed it, which was the most complicated part. She could feel the genuine weight of it across the kitchen, the way his shoulders carried it — but sorry was a category of information that had no operational function in the next forty-eight hours. "I need you to think about every single thing you said to Ames. Every call. Every implication. And I need you to tell me what he asked about that you didn't answer, because that's where Kane thinks the gaps are."

He straightened. The slump he'd been sitting in reorganized itself — not immediately, not like relief, but like someone picking up a thing they'd set down to say something difficult and now had to carry again.

"Okay," he said.

"And Daniel." She didn't move from the window. "I don't want to know your reasons anymore tonight. Do you understand that?"

He looked at her for a moment with an expression she didn't have a name for and didn't try to name.

"Yeah," he said. "I understand that."

The coffee was still on the counter. Still full. She pulled out her chair and sat back down, and laid her hands flat on the table the way he had, and said: "Start with the first call."
