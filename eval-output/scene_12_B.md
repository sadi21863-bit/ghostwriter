# Scene 12 — Version B

**Prompt:** Write a charged, vulnerable moment between Mira and Daniel in the safehouse after a near-miss with Kane's people.

**Word count:** 853

---

The lock clicked twice, then the deadbolt, and only then did Mira's hands stop.

She hadn't noticed them shaking until they weren't. She turned them over under the kitchen's single bulb, palms up, the way you'd check someone else's hands for injury. Steady now. Late. Her body had its own schedule and never consulted her.

Daniel came in behind her, shoulder first, the way he always entered a room — like he was clearing it for someone. He set the chain. Checked the window gap behind the blind without seeming to. She watched him do it and felt the old irritation that wasn't irritation at all, the thing she'd never bothered to name because naming it would have meant admitting it had a shape.

"That was close," he said.

"It wasn't close."

"Mira." He said her name like setting something down he'd been carrying too long. "The car turned onto the access road thirty seconds after we cleared it. That's close."

She poured water she didn't drink. The glass gave her hands a job. Across the room he was peeling off the jacket, and her eyes went to the long muscle of his forearm without her permission — the old scar there, the new tension in it — and she made herself look at the window instead. The looking had become a reflex she couldn't govern. She'd catch herself tracking the door for him to come through it. She'd lost a sentence to Reyes once, mid-briefing, because he'd rolled his sleeves and she had forgotten, briefly, entirely, what intelligence was.

This was the part she hated. The not-deciding. The way her attention kept arriving somewhere before her judgment had voted.

"You're doing the thing," he said.

"What thing."

"Where you stop being in the room."

She turned. He hadn't moved closer, which was its own kind of moving. He stood between her and the door, the way he stood between her and every door, and she'd told herself for two years it was tactical. Maybe it was. Maybe a person could mean two things with one body.

"You went back for the file," he said. Quiet. Not an accusation, which was worse. "The car was coming and you went back into the room for a folder."

"It's Priya's."

"I know whose it is." He scrubbed a hand down his face. The tired eyes got more tired. "That's the problem. You'll walk into the gun if the gun is standing in front of her. And I'll—" He stopped.

The water glass was very cold against her fingers. "You'll what."

"I'll be the one who let you."

It landed somewhere under her ribs and stayed there. Not warmth. Not the steady thing. Something with an edge, a current that ran up the back of her neck and made her aware of the distance between them as a measurable quantity, eight feet of bad linoleum she suddenly resented.

"You came back for me last time," she said. "After the leak. After everyone decided it was me. You were the only one who—"

"Don't." His voice cracked on it. "Don't make that a kindness. I came back and you were already three moves ahead and you didn't tell me any of them. You let me defend you to people who hated you for it without telling me what I was defending." He took one step. Just one. "I'm not asking you to need me. I learned that lesson. I'm asking you to not vanish into this the way she vanished. Because I can't run the docks twice."

The names hung there. Priya. Her. The same dark water.

She set the glass down because if she didn't she was going to break it, and she crossed the eight feet, and she didn't know what she was going to do until she had a fistful of his shirt and her forehead against his sternum and his heartbeat going too fast under her ear — fast like hers, she realized, fast like the car on the access road, both of them still running on the same chemical they hadn't agreed to.

His arms came around her slowly. Like she might be a trap. He'd earned that caution. She'd handed it to him.

"I don't know how to want something I might lose," she said into his shirt. Which was as close to it as she could get. Which was not close enough, and they both knew it, and neither of them said the word.

His chin came down to the top of her head. He didn't promise her anything. That was the thing about Daniel — he wouldn't say *I won't lose you* because he didn't make guarantees he couldn't keep, and she felt the absence of the lie like a hand on her spine, steadying.

"Then don't decide tonight," he said. "Just stay in the room."

She stayed in the room. His heart slowed against her cheek by degrees, and hers refused to follow, and somewhere out past the chained door a car that might have been theirs or might have been Kane's turned a corner and was gone.
