# Scene 05 — Version B

**Prompt:** Write the scene where Mira fights off two of Kane's security contractors at the Riverside Docks.

**Word count:** 1107

---

The fog comes off the river in slabs, the kind that swallows the sodium lights into orange halos and makes forty meters feel like ten. Mira counts the lights anyway. It is something to do with a mind that has been awake forty hours and keeps offering her things at the edge of sight — a man crouched by the gangway who is only a bollard, a voice that is only water slapping a hull. She wets her lips. Dry. The dock smells of rust and diesel and the green rot of the river, and somewhere under it, faintly, the cigarette one of them is smoking.

So there is at least one of them. The server logs said the manifest container had been here three weeks. The logs did not say Kane would have men on it.

She moves along the stacked containers, knife reversed against her forearm where it won't catch the light, edge out. Not a gun. A gun has a serial, and a serial is a story someone else writes about you. She told herself that in the safehouse and it sounded like discipline. Now, with her heartbeat in her ears and her vision pulling narrow, it sounds like the excuse it always was.

The cigarette man steps out of the gap between containers and she is already inside his reach.

He is big and he is reaching — not striking, reaching, hands coming up to take her by the shoulders, and that detail registers somewhere beneath thought even as her body answers. They want her whole. The realization is a half-second of cold clarity. She drops her weight and drives the heel of her free hand up under his sternum, all her hips behind it, and the breath goes out of him in a sound like a door. He folds. Not dramatic. The diaphragm spasms and a man simply cannot stand, cannot inhale, can only fish-mouth at the air while his body refuses him. He goes to his knees on the wet concrete and his phone clatters out of his jacket and skids.

Too fast. It is too fast, and the wrongness of it reaches her before the second man does.

He comes around the container with a baton already out and then he stops. Just for a moment. He looks at her — five-six, lean, a woman, the easy half of the job — and he recalculates, and that recalculation is a gift she did not have to earn. She closes the distance into the hesitation. The baton swings late and she is already past its arc, inside it, where it is just a length of dead steel against his own forearm. She traps the arm, hooks her foot behind his ankle, and turns. He goes down hard on his side and the baton rings away into the dark.

He's trained — he tries to post and scramble — but she's on the arm, and a person on the ground with a controlled limb has already lost most of the argument. He bucks. She rides it. Her thumb finds the pressure under his jaw and her knee pins the shoulder and he grunts, furious, and she feels in her own arms how easy it would be. A baton on the concrete an arm's length away. The knife. The river. Nobody on this dock who would write it down.

She gets the phone instead.

She has to release one point of control to do it — reaches back, drags the dropped phone toward her, thumbs it awake with a hand that won't quite steady. The screen is already on a photograph. A manifest. Container numbers, a date three weeks gone, a column of names in the casual bureaucratic font of people who file other people. And there, eleventh line down, *Nandi, P. — asset transfer.*

Asset transfer. Three weeks ago. Which means three weeks ago her sister was inventory, and inventory is something you move, and you do not move what is already dead.

The breath she takes goes in wrong, catches, and the man under her knee feels her attention slip and surges. They roll. Wet concrete grinds her shoulder, the phone bites into her palm — she does not let go of the phone — and then she has him again, worse for him this time, his face to the ground and her forearm across the back of his neck. He's breathing in ragged pulls. So is she now. The exertion has found her at last, thirty seconds of it, and her arms are shaking and the edge of her vision is graying and somewhere very far away she can hear herself deciding.

The mission is the photo. The photo is enough only if no one knows she has it. He has seen her face. He will describe her. He will tell Kane she was here, that she knows about the docks, that she is closer than Kane ever let himself believe, and the hearing is in days, and after the hearing everything locks.

Her grip is right. She knows exactly how. She has known how since a training mat a decade ago, before the agency decided she was the kind of person who leaked things, before she became the kind of person who would believe them about herself.

*You'll burn everyone,* Daniel said, the last time she didn't call him when she should have. *That's not a strategy, Mira. That's just who you'll be at the end of it.*

The man is saying something into the concrete. Curses, maybe. A name. She doesn't hear it — the sound has gone out of the world the way it does, leaving only her pulse and the slap of the river. Priya was alive three weeks ago. Priya, who wanted to do this the clean way, who thought the truth was a thing you handed to the right people and they did the rest.

Mira lets go.

She is up and moving before she can argue herself out of it, the phone crushed in her fist, her boots loud now on the concrete because quiet no longer matters. Behind her the man is coughing, pushing himself up on shaking arms, alive and conscious and already reaching for a radio she can hear crackle. She does not look back. The fog takes her between the containers and she runs, and it is only running, lungs sawing, the gray closing in, the choice she made riding her shoulders like a second body.

She has the proof. She has a witness who will tell Kane everything.

She doesn't know yet which of those is the mistake.
