# Scene 06 — Version B

**Prompt:** Write an atmospheric opening scene set in the abandoned dock warehouse at night, before anything happens.

**Word count:** 632

---

The fog came off the river in slow exhalations, not blown so much as breathed, rolling between the container stacks until the sodium lights overhead dissolved into floating coins of amber that lit nothing below them. Mira felt it before she saw any of it—the cold that wasn't quite cold, a wet weight that settled on the back of her neck and into the seams of her jacket, the river introducing itself through her skin. Somewhere ahead, steel groaned. A container swaying on its moorings, ten tons of it, rocking with a patience that made her teeth ache.

She crouched at the edge of the chain-link and let the place come to her in pieces.

Diesel first—the heavy, oily ground-note of any working yard, soaked into the asphalt so deep that thirty years of disuse wouldn't lift it. But under the diesel, threaded through it, something else. Sharp. Chemical. The bright ammoniac sting of refrigerant, and beneath *that*, faint and wrong, a sweetness that didn't belong to any cargo she wanted to think about. Her stomach tightened before her mind caught up. The body knew the smell of things kept cold for reasons. It always had.

Behind her, Daniel's breathing changed—the small adjustment of a big man trying to be small. She didn't turn.

"You're standing in my exit," she said.

"You don't have an exit. You have a fence and a prayer." His voice was low, swallowed by the fog almost before it reached her. "Which is why I'm here."

She'd told him not to come. She'd been precise about it, the way she was precise about everything she didn't want examined too closely, and he'd nodded the way he always did, the slow nod that meant nothing, and then he'd been at the laundromat door at nine with that look in his tired eyes that said *we both know how this goes.* The contradiction of him was that his loyalty felt like surveillance. He stood between her and every door, and she could never tell whether it was to protect her or to make sure she couldn't leave without him noticing.

She'd let him notice once before. It had cost him a partner and eighteen months of his life. He hadn't said that. He never said it. He just stood closer than he needed to.

Past the fence the yard opened into geometry—stacks of containers in long canyons, their corrugated walls beaded with condensation, the rust running down them in vertical stains like something had wept and dried. The light didn't reach the ground between them. The fog took it, smeared it, handed back only suggestion: the curve of a crane's neck two hundred yards out, the squared bulk of the warehouse, the wet shine of standing water in the low places where the asphalt had buckled.

It was quiet in the particular way that wasn't. Water against hulls, down at the wharf, a soft and ceaseless slapping. The metronome of the swaying container. The drip of the fog reorganizing itself off every surface. No engines. No voices. The yard performing emptiness the way the Foundation Tower performed transparency—a stillness so complete it felt staged.

"Place is dead," Daniel murmured. He wanted it to be true. She could hear the wanting in it.

Mira didn't answer. She was reading the puddle six feet ahead, where the sodium light found it and a thin film rode the surface—iridescent, fresh, the rainbow sheen of diesel that hadn't yet had time to wash gray. Old yards didn't make new oil. Dead places didn't leak.

She breathed in again, slow, and held the wrongness on her tongue. The refrigerant. The sweetness under it.

"No," she said finally, and her own voice surprised her, steady over the cold thing turning in her chest. "It isn't."
