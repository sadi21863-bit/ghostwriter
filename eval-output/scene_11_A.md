# Scene 11 — Version A

**Prompt:** Write the scene where Mira finally decodes the shell-company list and realizes it connects directly to Kane's confirmation hearing.

**Word count:** 1201

---

The laundromat's fluorescent tubes had been dying for three weeks, and Mira had stopped meaning to replace them. The light came in stutters now, a flicker she'd learned to read by — the way it caught on the dryer doors, the way it left the back corner in shadow where the door to the basement sat behind a stack of broken machines marked OUT OF ORDER in a hand that wasn't hers.

She went down. The stairs were slick. They were always slick.

The safehouse smelled of mildew and the gun oil she'd worked into the Sig that morning out of habit more than need, the rag still balled on the corner of the desk where Priya's printout lay flattened under a coffee mug. Six days. Then the ping. Then nine days of nothing, which was the part Mira did not let herself hold for too long, because holding it made her hands unsteady, and unsteady hands missed things.

She did not miss things. That was the one thing they could never actually take.

The shell-company list ran four pages, recovered off a drive Reyes shouldn't have had and definitely shouldn't have given her. Twelve entities. LLCs nested inside LLCs like a magician's boxes, registered agents in Delaware and Nevada and one in Wilmington that resolved to a UPS Store. She'd been at it since the laundromat opened upstairs, the rhythmic thud of someone's sneakers in the dryer keeping a kind of time.

Names. That was the trick of these things. People who built shells thought the names were noise — generic enough to slide past, vivid enough to feel real. Meridian Holdings. Blackcomb Asset Group. Vesper Maritime Services, registered to an address she'd already matched to a parking lot at the Riverside terminal, the same terminal where Priya's phone had surfaced for ninety seconds and then died.

She'd been treating the names as noise too. That was the mistake.

She pulled the legal pad toward her. Wrote them in a column, the way Priya used to organize everything — Priya, who color-coded her spice rack, who could never let a list stay a list. *You're not reading it,* Priya had told her once, years ago, over a kitchen table and a bottle they shouldn't have opened on a weeknight, *you're just looking at it. There's a difference, Mira. You of all people.*

She read it.

Meridian. Blackcomb. Vesper. Cantwell. Sable. Ardith. She wrote the first letters down the margin without deciding to, an old analyst's reflex, the way you doodle a pattern your eyes have already half-solved. M. B. V. C. S. A.

Nothing. She crossed it out.

Reordered them by registration date instead — earliest to latest, because money moved in sequence and sequence told you intent. The dates ran back nineteen months. The first three companies had been formed inside the same eleven-day window. She underlined the window. Then she set her pen down very carefully, the way you set down something you've realized is hot.

Nineteen months ago was the week the Foundation's surveillance contract went up for renewal. The week before the leak. The week before her name went into a file with the word *disloyal* attached to it by people who had never bothered to spell it correctly.

She opened her laptop and pulled the hearing schedule — public record, the Senate posted it like a menu. Kane's confirmation to the Intelligence oversight chair. Date set. Six weeks out.

Then she pulled the incorporation filing for Vesper Maritime, because Vesper was the one tied to the docks, and the docks were where Priya's signal had died, and she had been telling herself for nine days that those two facts lived in separate rooms.

The registered agent of record was a name she'd seen that morning and walked past, because it had been buried in the fine print of the third nested LLC, the kind of detail her eyes were trained to file as *clerical.* A. R. Cantwell. She'd assumed an attorney. There were Cantwells in every filing in the state; it was a clerk's name, a notary's name.

She typed it into the campaign finance database instead.

A. R. Cantwell had bundled four hundred thousand dollars for Kane's last campaign. Listed occupation: *consultant.* Listed employer: the Aldric Foundation.

Mira sat back. Upstairs, the dryer stopped, and the silence came down the stairwell like water finding a level.

It wasn't a surveillance program Kane was burying. A program was an embarrassment; an embarrassment you survived. You didn't form three shell companies in an eleven-day panic over an embarrassment. You didn't move things through a terminal at night for that. The shells weren't hiding the program. The shells were the thing the program had been built to *find* — a structure already standing, already moving money and cargo Mira hadn't traced yet, nineteen months before anyone leaked anything. The leak hadn't started this. The leak had threatened to *expose* something that was already running.

And the confirmation hearing wasn't the deadline because Kane wanted the job.

It was the deadline because the job came with the authority to make all of it legal. To classify the program he'd built. To seal the records. To put every shell, every terminal manifest, every ninety-second phone ping inside a vault marked *national security* and lose the key with full statutory cover, six weeks from now, in a hearing room with cameras and a flag.

After that, no one would be able to touch it. After that, anyone who already had would be a leak, and leaks got buried — she had the resume to prove it.

Her phone buzzed against the desk. Reyes. She let it go to the second ring before she answered, watching the screen, because answering on the first ring told people you were waiting.

"You found it," he said. Not a question. He always knew her by the quality of her quiet.

"Priya did," Mira said. "Nineteen months ago. She didn't go to the docks to disappear, Daniel. She went because that's where the answer was. She found it before I did."

A pause on his end, and in it she heard him doing the thing he did — standing up, moving toward a door, putting his body between her and whatever was coming next, even on a phone, even from across the city.

"Mira." His voice had gone careful. "If she found it nineteen months ago, then he's known for nineteen months that someone could."

She looked at the list. At the eleven-day window. At the hearing date, six weeks out, that was no longer a deadline she had time to beat so much as a wall closing the only exit.

"I know," she said.

She didn't say the rest, which was that the math ran both directions. That every day Priya had been worth keeping alive was a day Kane believed he hadn't yet found everything she'd taken. And that the hearing wasn't only his clock.

It was hers too. And it had started running the moment she finally read the page.

Upstairs, the laundromat door chimed. Someone had come in out of the dark, and the dying fluorescents stuttered once, twice, and held.
