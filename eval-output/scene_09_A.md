# Scene 09 — Version A

**Prompt:** Write the scene where Mira runs through the Foundation tower as the alarms go off, trying to reach the stairwell before security seals the floor.

**Word count:** 840

---

The alarm hit at 11:47, and the sound was wrong—not a bell but a synthesized tone, repeating, the pitch chosen by someone who understood that a frequency could feel like a hand on the spine. Mira was already moving. Fourteenth floor. The glass corridor stretched ahead of her, a hundred and twenty meters of transparency, the city smeared and orange beyond the curtain wall, and the stairwell door at the far end was a pale rectangle she had memorized from a floor plan that was already lying to her.

She ran.

Twenty meters in, her mouth went to paper. The dry-mouth came first, always first, the larynx tightening like a fist closing, and her breath turned ragged with a quickness that had nothing to do with the distance and everything to do with the chemistry flooding her. The corridor narrowed. Not the walls—her vision. The edges going soft and gray, everything not the door dropping away.

A *thunk* came up through the floor tiles behind her. Bass. She felt it in the soles of her feet before she understood it. The magnetic locks, engaging in sequence, sealing the corridor sections one by one at her back. Counting down toward her.

Forty meters. The elevator bank to her right spat light—doors opening, the soft chime obscene under the alarm, and the shapes of men in dark jackets unfolding out of it. Three of them. She didn't look. Looking cost time and her body would not spend it. Her arms pumped. The tone repeating, repeating, distorting into something that was no longer sound but pressure against the inner ear.

Sixty meters. The door resolving. And the door had a light on it.

Red.

The reinforced glass let her see the truth from forty meters out, the small unforgiving eye of the maglock already burning over the handle, and the floor plan in her head died in a single breath. *Already sealed.* The stairwell was gone. She had built her whole exit on that door and the door was a wall now and her legs were still carrying her toward it at full sprint with men closing behind.

The service elevator. Far corner. The other direction.

She planted, and the turn cost her everything at once—the momentum she'd built tearing through her knees, her shoe skidding on the polished floor, fifteen meters she would never get back peeling off the clock. The men saw her change. She heard one shout, muffled, underwater, the auditory exclusion swallowing the words before they reached her.

Run.

Lungs raw now. The cold air sawing. Her chest worked like a bellows with a hole in it and there was no breath left for thinking, only the corridor reversing past her, the city sliding the other way. *Thunk.* Closer. The locks gaining. Each one a section of floor she could no longer cross. The corridor was sealing toward her from both ends and she was the shrinking gap between.

Service elevator. Steel doors. Call button.

Her fingers found it and slid off—the cold, the shaking, the fine motor gone—and she hit it again with the flat of her hand. The doors held shut. One second. Two. Behind her the nearest *thunk* shuddered through the tile not ten meters back. Three. Four.

The doors parted.

She went sideways through the gap before they finished opening, hit the back wall, turned. Sub-basement. The lowest button, jabbed twice. Five seconds. The men were in the corridor now, the lead one's flashlight beam cutting white across the closing space, and his face turned toward hers across forty meters of dying corridor.

She knew that beam would find her.

It did. Six seconds. The light caught her full in the face and his stride broke—not because he was slow but because he *recognized* her, the analyst, the one they'd been told about, his mouth opening around her name as the doors began to slide.

Seven.

The gap narrowed to a blade of light, his face inside it, fixed on hers.

Eight. The doors met. The elevator dropped, and her stomach lifted, and somewhere far above her the last magnetic lock came down on the floor she'd just escaped with a sound she felt in her teeth.

She slid down the steel wall to the floor. The pain came in then—knees, lungs, the deferred bill arriving all at once now that there was nothing left to spend adrenaline on. Her hands were still shaking. Daniel had asked her, two nights ago in the flooded dark of the safehouse, *promise me you don't go in alone.* She had said something that was not a promise and let him believe it was.

The numbers fell. Twelve. Eleven. Ten.

They'd known she was coming. The architecture was tighter than her intelligence had said, the door sealed before she reached it, the men already in the elevator. Someone had told them. Someone always told them.

Nine. Eight.

She got her feet under her before the doors opened, because whatever was waiting in the sub-basement, she would meet it standing.
