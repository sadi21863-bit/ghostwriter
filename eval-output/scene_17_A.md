# Scene 17 — Version A

**Prompt:** Write a scene establishing the Aldric Foundation Tower from the inside, through Mira's eyes, on her first visit.

**Word count:** 703

---

The smell hit her before the lobby did: filtered air scrubbed of everything, the absence of smell engineered so completely it became its own scent, the way a recording of silence still hisses. Mira's nostrils flared anyway, hunting. Years of buildings that announced themselves — wet plaster, mouse droppings behind drywall, the ammonia bloom of a public bathroom doing its honest work. This place had been sterilized of all that. She didn't trust a building that hid what it was made of.

She stopped just inside the revolving door and let the others flow past her toward the desk. The lobby went up — not a ceiling so much as a confession of vertical wealth, a glass shaft that pulled the eye upward until the brain gave out. Light fell from everywhere and nowhere. No shadows to hide in, which meant no shadows for anyone else either, but Mira's body didn't read it that way. Her body read open ground, no cover, every sightline owning her at once. She drifted left without deciding to, toward the planter wall, until her shoulder found the cold travertine and her back had something behind it.

The floor was polished to a depth that showed her standing on her own reflection. She hated walking on her own face.

Two security men flanked the elevator bank, hands folded at the belt, the posture of men paid to be furniture until they weren't. She catalogued them — exits behind them, the service corridor to the right that nobody had bothered to disguise, the camera in the upper corner whose little black dome turned the whole atrium into a single watched eye. Counting exits was a thing her hands did before her mind caught up. She'd been doing it since the apartment on Carrow Street, since the night the only door had been the window. She didn't think about Carrow Street. She just always knew where the second way out was.

"Ms. Okonkwo?" The receptionist had a smile assembled from a manual. "Mr. Aldric is expecting you. Fourteenth floor."

Fourteenth. Not the top. Mira filed it. People who wanted to intimidate put you on the top floor and made you watch the city shrink. People who wanted to keep you put you in the middle, where you couldn't see how far down it was.

The elevator was a glass coffin that ran up the inside of the shaft, and she made herself step into it because hesitating would be noticed, and being noticed was worse than being afraid. As it rose, the lobby fell away beneath her boots — that drop, that nothing under her — and her stomach climbed her throat. She fixed her eyes on the brushed-steel panel, on the seam where the doors met, on anything that wasn't the void. A part of her, the part she could never quite kill, registered that the view was beautiful. The city laid out gold and gray in the late light, the river a slow muscle through it. She wanted to look. That was the trap. They built it so you'd want to look, so you'd want to belong to the height, so the wanting would do the work no guard could.

She wanted in. That was the whole problem. She had spent her life learning every building so she could leave it fast, and now she stood in the most controlled space she'd ever entered, wanting to be let further in. Wanting Aldric's signature, his money, his name fastened to her work. The two impulses sat in her at the same time, neither winning — the door at her back, and the room she was walking toward.

The fourteenth floor exhaled when the doors opened. Warmer air. And there — under the engineered nothing — the faintest thread of cigar smoke gone stale in upholstery, weeks old, the smell of a man who made his own rules in a building that allowed none. It went straight past her thinking and landed somewhere older. Her father's coat. The back of a car she shouldn't have been in.

She stepped out and, without looking, found the corner of the room with her eyes, and chose the chair that faced the door.
