# Scene 07 — Version B

**Prompt:** Write the scene where Mira waits in the dark of her safehouse, certain Kane's car is about to pull up outside.

**Word count:** 850

---

The car would come from the south. Mira knew this the way she knew most things now—not from evidence but from the absence of it. The laundromat upstairs had closed at nine. The dryers had stopped their tumbling at ten. For the last forty minutes the only sound through the floor had been the building settling, and she had counted each creak the way she used to count breaths during surveillance.

She sat with her back to the water-stained wall, where she could see both the door and the narrow window that looked out at ankle height onto the alley.

The phone in her hand showed 10:47.

She had given Reyes the address two days ago. That was the problem with safehouses. They were only safe until someone you trusted knew where they were, and after that they were just rooms.

Outside, a car passed. She tracked the wash of its headlights across the ceiling—the angle, the speed, the way the light pooled and slid and was gone. North to south. Too fast. Not it.

She let her shoulders down by a degree.

The basement smelled of mildew and the oil she'd run through the Beretta that afternoon, a smell she'd stopped noticing until silence made her notice everything. There was a drip somewhere behind her, in the dark she didn't look at. It had been dripping for three days. It would drip whether Kane came or not.

He would come himself. She was almost sure of that. A man like Kane didn't send people for the things that mattered to him; he'd told her as much without telling her, in the way he'd looked at her across the Foundation lobby last week, unhurried, the silver head tilting like he was reading the small print of her. *I know what you are,* that look had said. *I have time.*

That was the part she couldn't sit with. The time. He had all of it. She had Priya's ping near the docks, six days cold, and the math that got worse every hour, and a man downtown who could afford to wait because waiting cost him nothing.

10:49.

Another car. She watched the light. South to north this time, slow, slowing—and her hand was already flat on the Beretta on the floor beside her, fingers reading it without her eyes—and the light swept the ceiling and stopped.

The engine idled.

She did not move. She had learned how to not move from people who'd done it better than her, in places she wasn't allowed to name on official paper. You let the body go quiet. You let the heart do what it wanted because you couldn't stop it anyway, and you breathed shallow through the mouth so the breath made no sound, and you waited for the thing to declare itself.

The idle held. Ten seconds. Fifteen.

She thought about the door. Three steps to it, the chain she'd put up that wouldn't hold a determined shoulder for more than a second, the laundromat above with its dark windows and its smell of detergent, and beyond that the street where a car was sitting with its engine running at ten forty-nine at night in a district where no one parked because there was nothing to park for.

A car door opened. She heard it through the window, the particular soft clunk of it, and then a voice—

"—no, tell him I'll call back, I'm three minutes out—" A man's voice. Tired. The slap of a takeout bag against a thigh. Footsteps going the other way, up the alley, into a building that wasn't hers.

The engine pulled off. The light slid down the wall and emptied the room of itself.

Mira breathed out, long, through her teeth.

Her hand was still on the gun. She made herself take it off, finger by finger, and set it down. The drip went on behind her. 10:51 now. She had been holding the same posture so long her right leg had gone numb from the hip, and she stretched it out across the cold floor and felt the blood come back like static.

He hadn't come. Not tonight.

But the thing about a man who had all the time was that you spent yours waiting for him, and she understood, sitting there with her pulse finally slowing and the gun oil sharp in her nose again, that this was the shape of it now—that she would sit in this room every night and watch the ceiling for his headlights, and he would let her, because every night she gave him she was a night closer to giving up on Priya, and he knew it, and he could afford the wait.

She didn't turn on the light.

She picked the phone back up, and she texted Reyes one word—*docks*—and she watched the screen until it said *delivered*, and then she sat in the dark and waited for it to say *read*.

It didn't.

She watched the window. North to south, the next car came, and the light moved across the ceiling, and she counted it.
