# Scene 04 — Version A

**Prompt:** Write the conversation where Mira confronts Senator Kane directly for the first time, in his office.

**Word count:** 1509

---

The office smelled of cedar and something older — the particular staleness of rooms where decisions are made and the air doesn't circulate. Mira had been waiting eleven minutes when Kane came through the side door, not the main one, which meant he'd been informed and chose the entrance anyway. A small thing. She noted it.

He was taller than his photographs suggested. The photographs always managed to convey importance without conveying size, but in person he occupied the room the way certain men did — not aggressively, just completely, like furniture that had always been there.

"Ms. Voss." He said it the way he might announce a weather system. Informational. Neither welcoming nor hostile.

Her hands were cold. They'd been cold since the elevator, which she'd noticed and filed. The cold had nothing to do with the temperature.

"Senator." She stayed where she was, which was standing in front of his desk rather than beside it, the small territorial claim she'd staked while waiting. He moved around her to his chair without appearing to notice, which meant he noticed completely.

He sat. He didn't gesture to the chair across from him.

"I have a committee briefing in—" he checked nothing, no watch, no phone— "twenty-two minutes."

"That's enough time," she said.

The inner corners of her brows had pulled fractionally upward — the involuntary AU1 that had preceded tears when she was twenty-two and hadn't preceded anything since. She'd trained it down to a twitch. It was twitching now.

"Is it." He folded his hands on the desk. His voice carried the full modulation of a man whose nervous system had not moved out of a resting state. Unhurried. The full prosodic range — rise and fall, the consonants finished — of someone who had not registered this as a threat. Or was performing not registering it, which was the same thing at this distance.

She said, "Priya Nandi."

The name landed. She watched for it.

Nothing moved in his face. That was the tell — the absence rather than the presence. A man who'd never heard the name would ask. A man with nothing to hide would ask. His brow held its position, the corrugator unengaged, and he looked at her with the particular blankness of someone who had rehearsed not reacting and was now performing the rehearsal.

"I'm not familiar with—"

"She was a contracted analyst on the Meridian review." Mira's voice was flat, breath-supported from the lower chest, slower than her resting rate because she was controlling it. "She contacted your office four times in the fourteen days before she went dark. The last contact was a voicemail your scheduler logged as received."

Something crossed Kane's face that was gone before she could fully catalogue it. The chin raiser — AU17, the muscle beneath the lower lip — a flicker, half a second, less. It was the expression of someone who had not expected the specific shape of what had just been said.

He recovered into stillness. "I receive a significant volume of correspondence through my office. I can't be expected to—"

"She mentioned the docks." 

Mira said it without inflection. It was not a question. It was not an accusation. It was a fact placed on the desk between them, and she watched him decide what to do with it.

His hands, folded on the desk, did not move. This too was a performance — the effort not to move them was visible in the slight tension across his knuckles. His voice, when it came, had dropped a register. Barely. The prosody had narrowed — the vowels flatter, the consonants fractionally clipped — the voice the body produces when the sympathetic system begins its quiet activation.

"Ms. Voss." He said her name differently this time. The weather system had arrived. "You were dismissed from the agency under a formal finding. I'm sure you understand that your presenting yourself in my office with—"

"I'm not presenting anything." She took one step toward the desk. Not toward him. Toward the desk. "I'm asking whether you know where she is."

"I don't know who she is."

"You just said you receive significant correspondence. You didn't say you didn't know her."

His eyes held hers for two seconds past the point of social comfort. His brows — she watched the specific muscles — held level. Not the AU4 of sustained threat-engagement, not yet. He was still, she realized, deciding what she had. He was running probability.

A different kind of cold moved through her chest. Not her hands this time. The body registering something the mind was three seconds behind.

He knew. He had known for some time and the thing he was currently deciding was not whether she was telling the truth but whether she already had enough to matter.

"I'd like you to leave," he said. It was quiet. Quieter than anything he'd said so far. The vocal energy pulled back, the prosody flattening not into hostility but into something that was its own register entirely — the flat, fully supported tone of someone exercising a decision they've already finished making.

"My sister." The word came out of her without the architecture she'd built around it. She heard the shift in her own voice — the breath support had dropped, the pitch had risen a quarter-tone without her permission — and she locked it back down before the next word. "Is missing. And you know something about why."

The AU17 in his chin had not reappeared. What had appeared instead, gradually, with the patience of a man who had been through harder rooms than this, was something she could only catalog as sorrow — not performed, not strategic — the real thing sitting behind his eyes in a way that made it worse, not better, because it meant he felt it and had chosen anyway.

"I know," he said carefully, "that you were a talented analyst before the Meridian matter. I know that what happened to you was—" he paused, and the pause was genuine, she could hear the breath in it— "not uncomplicated."

"Don't."

"And I know that when we lose someone—"

"She isn't lost." The cold in her chest had sharpened to something specific, a point below the sternum. "She's somewhere. Someone put her there."

Kane was quiet for a moment. His hands unfolded, slowly, and he placed them flat on the desk — an opening gesture, palms down rather than gripped, the body offering something the voice hadn't yet. She watched the AU6, the faint engagement of the orbicularis oculi, the crow's-foot pull that could not be voluntarily produced. It appeared and dissolved so quickly she wasn't certain she'd seen it.

"Twenty-two minutes ago," he said, "you walked into this building. You've been on a watch list since March. Two men in that lobby have photographs of you. You think I don't know what you're doing, Ms. Voss — coming here, in person, without anything hard enough to use." His voice had not risen. "You're hoping I'll give you something."

"I already have something."

"Then use it."

She looked at him. The room was very quiet. Outside, the ambient hum of the building's ventilation system registered as a presence rather than sound.

"You'll be in confirmation hearings in nine days," she said. "That's enough time."

His brow engaged. The AU4 — the corrugator pulling the brows down and together, the specific configuration of the face assembling around a serious threat calculation, genuine and not performed. He held it for a beat and then let it go, and what was underneath it, briefly visible, was something she had not expected.

He looked tired.

Not panicked. Not calculating. Just tired, the way a man looked who had been carrying a specific weight for a specific amount of time and was beginning to understand that carrying it was no longer the whole problem.

"Find what you need to find," he said quietly. "And then ask yourself what it costs."

"I know what it costs."

"No." Something shifted in his face — the corrugator released, the brows settling into a flatness that wasn't neutral but was done, the face of a man closing a door. "You know what you're willing to pay. Those aren't the same number."

She held his gaze for three seconds. Her hands were no longer cold. They had moved past cold into the particular numbness that sat on the other side of it, the body's conversion of dread into something more operational.

She picked up her bag from the chair she'd never been offered.

"I'll show myself out," she said.

He didn't answer. She walked to the door — the main door, not the side door he'd come through — and when she reached it she didn't look back, which cost her something she didn't name until she was in the elevator, watching the floor numbers change, her reflection fragmented in the brushed steel doors.

The AU17 in her chin was working steadily now, with no one to perform for.

She let it.
