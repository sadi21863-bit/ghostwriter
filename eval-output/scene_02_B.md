# Scene 02 — Version B

**Prompt:** Write the scene where Mira breaks into the Foundation's archive room looking for the shell-company list.

**Word count:** 1181

---

The archive room was three floors below the lobby, past two checkpoints that required badges Mira didn't have and a service corridor that smelled of recycled air and cleaning solution. She'd come through the HVAC access shaft instead, dropping the last six feet in silence, landing in a crouch that sent a familiar ache up through her left knee—the souvenir from Beirut that no personnel file would ever name.

Thirty-eight minutes.

The room was smaller than the floor plan suggested. Foundation blueprints filed with the city showed a standard records annex, shelving units, a dehumidifier in the corner. What the blueprints didn't show was the secondary vault door at the far end, matte steel with a biometric panel glowing amber in the dark. Mira had expected the door. She hadn't expected how quiet the room was—how the climate-control hum swallowed her own breathing until she couldn't hear herself think, which was, she supposed, appropriate.

She crossed to the panel in eight steps and crouched in front of it. The thumbprint cartridge was in her jacket's inner pocket, a half-centimeter silicone shell cast from an impression she'd lifted off a water glass at the Foundation's April donor reception. Marcus Hale, deputy director, had a habit of holding his glass by the bowl—a nervous tell she'd catalogued six weeks before anyone told her she shouldn't be cataloguing anything anymore. The gel was cold against her index finger when she pressed it into the cast, colder when she pressed the whole assembly to the reader, and for a long moment nothing happened except the hum of the climate system and the distant sound of her own pulse.

The panel shifted from amber to green.

She was inside in four seconds.

The vacuum-sealed boxes were stacked floor to ceiling on industrial shelving, organized by fiscal year in a numbering system that meant nothing until she'd spent eleven days memorizing the Foundation's internal audit shorthand. Mira moved by feel and by the narrow beam of a penlight held in her teeth, scanning labels, pulling boxes in and returning them, her hands moving with a deliberateness that looked almost calm. She'd been told, once, by a supervisor she didn't respect, that she worked like a surgeon. She'd thought at the time he meant it as a compliment. She understood now that he'd meant it as a warning.

The ledger was in the fourth box she opened.

Not a digital file. Actual paper—the old kind, dense and cream-colored, forty years of slow acid turning it brittle at the edges. The smell came up immediately, something dry and faintly sweet, like a room no one had opened a window in since before she was born. She turned pages with her thumbnail, careful not to bend anything, reading shell company names by penlight: transit addresses in the Caymans, port logistics firms with no employee records, three separate LLCs with Foundation board members buried four layers deep in the ownership chain. She photographed each page with her phone, steady hand, no flash, the click of the shutter muffled against her palm.

Then the note fell out.

It was folded into the back cover, not filed, not archived—tucked, like someone had run out of time to do anything else with it. A piece of plain white paper, torn at the bottom where it had been pulled fast from a spiral-bound notebook. Mira recognized the handwriting before she'd fully unfolded it. She'd grown up watching that handwriting on birthday cards, on grocery lists, on the edges of textbooks that Priya had never been able to stop annotating.

*They know I know. If something happens check the transit ledger. 3rd column, March routing.*

The date was in the upper right corner, small and careful, the way Priya always dated things because she'd read somewhere that undated evidence was inadmissible. Three days before she went dark. Mira stared at it until the penlight started to shake and she made herself stop doing that, made herself breathe through her nose the way she'd been trained to breathe when something was trying to open up inside her chest.

Priya had stood in this room.

Not outside it, not near it—*in* it. Had held this ledger. Had understood exactly what she was holding and known, already, that the understanding was going to cost her something she hadn't priced yet. The note wasn't frightened. That was the thing. Mira read it again and the handwriting was deliberate, level, the same hand that had annotated those textbooks. Priya had written it the way she did everything—like she was taking notes for someone who came after her.

Twenty-one minutes.

Mira folded the note and slipped it into the inside pocket of her jacket, separate from the ledger, which she tucked flat against her spine under her belt. She photographed the box as she'd found it. She photographed the shelf. She closed the vault door until she heard the seal catch, checked the panel, and was back through the service corridor in four minutes, moving quickly now, moving because if she stopped she would think about the handwriting and she couldn't afford to think about the handwriting yet.

Reyes was where she'd left him—in the alcove beside the loading dock, hood up, hands in his pockets in the particular way that meant he'd been watching three directions at once for the last half hour. The exit route he'd cleared was still technically jammed; she could see the wedge of shadow where the fire door hadn't fully latched. She'd managed through a secondary egress anyway. She always did.

He looked at her face when she came through and said nothing. That was Reyes. He could read her the way you read a weather system—not what it was, but what it meant was coming. His jaw set.

She stopped in front of him and the sodium light from the dock turned everything orange and flat, and she met his eyes because he deserved that much, because he'd stood out here in the cold for thirty-eight minutes for her and had not once in the last six weeks asked her to promise anything she wouldn't keep.

She didn't tell him about the note. She didn't tell him about the ledger, or the March routing column, or the date in the upper right corner written in her sister's careful hand. She didn't tell him what she'd already started calculating on the walk back through the service corridor—which thread to pull next, which bridge to burn getting there, how many of the people who trusted her she could afford to keep trusting her back.

Reyes looked at her for a long moment. Then he looked away, out at the dock, at the fog sitting low over the water, at nothing.

"Car's around the south corner," he said.

"I know," she said.

He pulled the fire door shut behind them as they walked, and the sound of it latching carried across the water like something final, and neither of them spoke again, because there wasn't anything left to say that wouldn't make it worse to have said it.
