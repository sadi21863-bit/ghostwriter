# Scene 15 — Version B

**Prompt:** Write the scene where Mira interrogates a captured Foundation operative about Priya's location.

**Word count:** 1284

---

The basement smelled of rust and standing water. A pipe somewhere behind the breaker panel had given out hours ago, and the concrete underfoot held a thin sheet of it that caught the light when the bulb overhead stuttered. It flickered in no pattern Mira could name, except that her eyes had started reading it like code anyway. Old habit. The agency had trained her to find signal in everything, and then thrown her out for finding it where they didn't want it found.

The operative sat with his back to the support column, wrists zip-tied to the chair's frame. Forties, gym-built, the kind of man who'd been told he was good at silence. He was watching her the way you watch weather.

She didn't sit across from him. She pulled a second chair, turned it, and set it a little to his side, so he had to crane to keep her in view. Then she said nothing for long enough that the dripping pipe became the only voice in the room.

"You drove the white Transit," she said finally. Not a question. "Pier 9, the loading apron. You backed it to the water so the cameras caught the cab and not the bay doors." She let that land. "I'm not interested in the van."

His jaw worked once. "Then we're done."

"We're not done, because you keep looking at the door behind me, and there's nothing behind that door but more water." She leaned back. "Help me understand it from where you were standing. You get a call. You don't ask who's in the back of a van you're told not to open. That's not loyalty. That's a man who's been doing this long enough to know which questions get you killed."

"You'd know about getting killed for questions."

The flicker hit the wet floor and threw light up across his knuckles. They were white. He'd gripped the chair edge the second she said *Pier 9*, and he hadn't let go.

She noticed. She didn't name it. That was the discipline—you let the tell sit in the room like a fourth person and you let him feel it being witnessed.

"My sister was in that van," Mira said. The word *sister* cost her something and she spent it deliberately, because grief reads as leverage to men like him and she needed him to think he had it. "I already know that part. What I want to understand is the handoff. Who took receipt."

Silence. She'd left a gap shaped exactly like an answer and waited to see what crawled into it.

"You think there was a handoff," he said.

It was nothing. It was everything. *You think.* Not *I won't tell you.* Not *there wasn't.* A man protecting a location says *I don't know where she is.* A man who genuinely doesn't know corrects your premise.

She turned the chair a degree, slow, and his eyes tracked it.

"Let me tell you what I know, so we don't waste each other's evening." She counted on her fingers without urgency. "I know the van. I know the pier. I know the timestamp. I know there's no second vehicle on any feed leaving that apron, which means whatever went into the water went in by boat or didn't leave at all." She watched his breathing change—a half-second hitch on *the water*. "And I know you're sitting here doing math, and the math is: she can't put me anywhere worse than Kane already can. You're right. I can't. He owns every cell I could put you in. So I'm not here to threaten you with a future. I'm here because you already know how this ends and I'm the only person in your life who's going to ask you to say it out loud."

His head dropped. When he spoke it was lower, the cocky register gone, and that frightened her more than defiance would have.

"You don't get it. There's no *where*." He swallowed. "That's not how he runs it. You think there's a—a room with a door and a guard and a name on a roster. There's no roster. The asset protocol—"

He stopped himself. Too late. The phrase had a shape to it, a procedure, a thing decided in advance.

"The asset protocol," Mira repeated, gently, the way you'd repeat a child's word back to them so they kept going.

"I don't know where she is." His knuckles flexed against the tie. "I'm telling you the truth and it's the worst thing I could tell you. Nobody knows where she is. Compartments. I move what I'm told, I don't get the next link. The next link doesn't get me. That's the *point*. So there's no thread you pull, because he didn't build a thread. He built—" the bulb stuttered, and the wet floor threw its broken light up into his face— "He built a room nobody can walk out of and then he forgot which room. On purpose."

The pipe dripped. Once. Twice.

She had come here for a place. A grid reference, a warehouse, a hold time. Something to drive to fast enough. And she understood now, sitting in the rising damp with this frightened, useless man, that the place had stopped existing as a place the moment Kane invoked a *protocol*—a word you only reach for when the decision is already made and you want it to feel like weather instead of murder. *The asset protocol.* You don't protocol someone you're keeping. You protocol someone you've decided to lose.

Not hours ago. Weeks.

He'd disappeared Priya the way you file a thing you never intend to retrieve, and everyone since had only been moving the absence around so it couldn't be traced back to the man who'd authored it.

"He decided before the leak," she said, and it wasn't to him.

"What?"

"You said you don't get the next link." She stood. Slowly. The chair scraped through the water. "But you know the protocol exists. Which means you know what it's *for*. And what it's for is making sure nobody ever has to know where she—"

"Listen to me." Something in him broke open, and it was almost grief, almost relief, the awful gratitude of a man finally allowed to confess. "If you want, I can tell you who gave me the call. I can give you the night, I can give you the—just don't walk out, I can give you—"

She was already at the door.

She left him there, mouth still moving, the offer still in the air, because he had finally given her the one true thing he had and it was not a location. It was that there wasn't one. He could hand her every name in the chain and not one of them would lead to a living woman, because the chain hadn't been built to hold a person—only to dissolve her.

Behind her his voice rose and then the door took it. The bulb flickered its broken message at the wet floor, to no one.

She'd come down here to find her sister.

She climbed the stairs understanding she'd been six days late before the van ever moved—and that the only thing left to recover was the answer to *why*, and the name of the man who'd already decided she'd have to live with it.

Daniel was waiting at the top, where she'd told him not to be. He read her face the way he read everything—too fast, too completely.

"You went in without me," he said. Not the accusation. The one underneath it. *What did you do down there.*

Mira didn't answer that either. She'd gotten good, lately, at the gaps shaped like answers.
