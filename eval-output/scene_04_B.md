# Scene 04 — Version B

**Prompt:** Write the conversation where Mira confronts Senator Kane directly for the first time, in his office.

**Word count:** 1533

---

The white-noise machine started the moment she stepped past the aide, which told her everything about the kind of conversations this office was designed to contain.

Kane didn't look up. He was signing documents at his desk — not pretending to sign them, actually signing them, the pen moving in steady arcs across the signature line of one page and then the next, a man disposing of business before dealing with the interruption she represented. The desk was mahogany, the old kind, not veneer, and it was wide enough that the distance between them felt architecturally deliberate. On the near corner, a framed photograph lay face-down. She clocked it without moving her eyes from him. She'd learned that from Daniel, years before she stopped listening to the things Daniel taught her.

"Ms. Voss," Kane said, still writing.

Her chest registered his voice before her mind sorted the syllables. A tightening — not fear, the body making fear's preparatory gesture without quite committing to it. She stood with her weight distributed, hands loose, and waited for him to finish performing indifference.

When he finally set the pen down, he looked at her the way she imagined he looked at weather: as a condition to be managed, not a thing with its own agenda.

"You've gone to considerable effort to get to this desk," he said. "That deserves acknowledgment, at least."

His voice was a register lower than she'd expected from the confirmation hearings she'd watched. In front of cameras he pitched warmer — the prosody of a man demonstrating that he listened, the deliberate Duchenne crinkle that arrived at the corner of his eyes just before his smile. Here, there was none of that machinery running. Just the lower frequency, the even pace. More honest. More dangerous.

"I know about the secondary layer," Mira said.

Not an opening — a placement. She watched the muscle above his jaw. Nothing moved.

"The surveillance architecture." She let it sit in the air between them, where it would either catch something or it wouldn't. "Not the program that got disclosed. The one that ran parallel. Different classification ceiling. Different compartment. Different purpose."

Kane picked up his pen again, not to write — she understood that — but because men like him needed to hold something when they were making a decision about how much to spend. He turned it once between his fingers. Once was enough for him.

"That's a meaningful allegation," he said. "From a woman whose access was revoked fourteen months ago."

"Revoked after a leak I didn't commit."

"That's what people who commit leaks say." He said it without inflection, without cruelty. As a fact about the world.

Her hands were cold. They'd been cold since the parking garage, since she'd sat in the car deciding whether to walk through the door or keep the information controlled and distant where it couldn't yet destroy her. She'd walked through the door. The cold hadn't gone anywhere.

"Priya Nandi," she said. "I'd like you to tell me where she is."

Something crossed his face — not flinching, not the AU17 chin-raiser of suppressed grief, nothing so legible. A fractional stillness, the way a person stills when they've determined to produce no motion rather than actually being still. It lasted less than a second and he was moving again, setting the pen down properly this time, aligning it with the edge of a document.

"I don't know anyone by that name."

"You knew her when she was a program analyst on the Foundation's data architecture review. You knew her when she started asking questions about the access logs. You knew her name very specifically when you had her credentialing reviewed by someone outside her supervisory chain." Mira heard her own voice as she was speaking it — flatter than she wanted, the prosody shaved down by the effort of keeping everything else in place. She let a breath in through her nose and continued. "You've known her name for eight months. I'd like to know where she is now."

Kane stood. He was taller than the cameras suggested, and the standing was deliberate — not aggression, recalibration. He moved around the side of the desk the way someone moves in a space they own completely, and stopped at the window that looked out over the street below. His back was partially to her. Another kind of information.

"Your sister," he said finally, "made herself difficult to protect."

Mira's sternum did something that wasn't a sound, that happened entirely inside the cage of her ribs. The James-Lange sequence running its honest sequence: body first, the stun of it, the cold racing from her hands up through her forearms before her mind assembled what he'd just done — *your sister*, acknowledging the relationship, acknowledging *Priya*, the entire facade of non-recognition folded and discarded in one subordinate clause, and what replaced it so much worse than the denial had been.

*Made herself.* Past tense.

"She was managing a risk that wasn't hers to manage," Kane said, to the window, to the city below it. "Ambitious people often mistake their access for their authorization. The program she was looking at served a function that she didn't have the full context to evaluate."

"Where is she." Not a question this time. The grammar of it changed by will.

"The inquiry you're conducting—" he turned back to face her, and his expression was the expression of a man explaining something to someone who isn't going to accept the explanation, which meant he'd already arrived somewhere past her acceptance, "—has a ceiling. I'd suggest you understand where it is before you reach it."

"That's a threat."

"It's a description," he said, and the evenness of it was worse than heat would have been, worse than the kind of menace that comes in with its volume up. "You've worked in a threat environment. You know the difference. I'm describing a condition. The condition is: there are people ahead of the confirmation hearing who have spent considerable effort managing a disclosure that could have damaged this institution. People who are, at this moment, not looking at you. I'm not in a position to guarantee that remains true."

He said *institution* and she heard *himself.* She'd worked with people who believed they were the institution they served. It wasn't vanity, which was why it was so hard to argue against. Kane was looking at her with something that was nearly clinical, nearly interested — the inner brows very slightly contracted, AU4 sustained, the expression of a man trying to accurately assess a variable he didn't fully choose to have in the room.

"What I hear," Mira said carefully, "is that you're managing exposure. Which means the program is still running."

A beat. The pen was on the desk. The photograph was face-down on the desk. The white-noise machine was behind her, beyond the door.

"What I hear," she said, when he didn't answer, "is that my sister went dark six days ago, and you just told me why."

"I told you nothing about your sister." He said it precisely. Too precisely — the excessive precision of a man putting down a marker he could point to later.

"No," she said. "You told me she made herself a liability. That she was managing risk that wasn't hers." She felt the cold in her hands reaching up toward her shoulders now, felt the muscle in her throat that wanted to close and was not going to. "Those aren't past tense because you've lost track of her."

Kane walked back behind his desk with the same unhurried economy, sat down, and picked up the pen. He looked at the document in front of him. When he spoke it was to the document and not to her, the dismissal contained in the direction of his gaze.

"You should take whatever you believe you've uncovered," he said, "and consider carefully who it puts at risk. Not theoretically. Concretely. Name by name."

She stood there long enough to establish it was a choice that she left, not an expulsion, and then she walked out through the anteroom without looking at the aide, without looking at the door that the aide held open, and she got as far as the elevator bank before she stopped moving.

The marble was cold through her jacket where she leaned against the wall. Her hands were shaking now — not trembling, shaking, the distinction her body was making clear without consultation. Down the corridor the white-noise machine continued its hum, inaudible from here but present in some way she couldn't account for, its frequency inside her chest where the tightening had started and hadn't finished.

*Made herself difficult to protect.*

She took out her phone. Daniel's name sat in the recent contacts like an accusation, and she thought about the last conversation — the one where she'd told him she just needed a day, the one where *a day* was already not true when she said it. She pressed call before the argument she was constructing against it could complete itself.

He picked up on the second ring.

She said: "I need to tell you what I did."
