# Scene 17 — Version B

**Prompt:** Write a scene establishing the Aldric Foundation Tower from the inside, through Mira's eyes, on her first visit.

**Word count:** 986

---

The smell hit her before the doors finished sighing shut behind her: lavender and bergamot pumped through some hidden vent, the kind of scent engineered in a lab to mean *calm, you are safe, you are welcome.* It was the wrong smell. Underneath it, faint and persistent, was something Mira knew from rooms she wasn't supposed to remember—the metallic tang of industrial air handling running too hard, ozone and scrubbed steel, the chemistry of a building that breathed for you whether you wanted it to or not. The lavender was the lie. The metal was the truth, and her body had already decided which one to believe; her shoulders had dropped half an inch toward a fighting stance before her conscious mind caught up.

She did not stop walking. Stopping was a behavior. Stopping got logged.

The lobby was a cathedral built to be photographed. Three stories of glass curtain wall poured the gray morning straight onto a floor of pale stone, and everything—the reception desk, the floating staircase, the visitors drifting between them—was lit as if for an audience that wasn't there. *Transparency,* the architecture announced. *We have nothing to hide. Look anywhere you like.* Mira had read enough rooms to understand that a space designed to let you see everything was a space designed to watch you do the looking. There were no corners to stand in. No alcove, no column wide enough to put your back against. The openness wasn't generosity. It was a field of fire with the furniture removed.

She mapped it the way she'd been trained and couldn't stop doing even now, even disgraced, even six months out from a clearance she'd never get back. Two cameras she could see, domed and smoked, mounted to read faces at the threshold. Four she couldn't, which meant six, which meant the lobby was solved before anyone reached the desk. The reception staff wore the same soft gray as the walls. A man in a charcoal suit stood near the staircase doing nothing at all, hands loose, weight even, and the *nothing* was the tell. Idle people leaned. They checked phones, shifted, scratched. This one held himself like a man waiting to be needed, and that economy of stillness was a thing you had to be taught.

Mira chose the slowest version of herself. She let her gaze go soft and middle-distanced, a woman with an appointment she was mildly anxious about, the most forgettable creature in any glass building in the world. She did not look at the cameras. Looking at cameras was what guilty people and tourists did, and she intended to be neither—just badge-less, just a name on a visitor list she'd talked her way onto through a contractor's portal three days ago, a name belonging to a real catering company that really did service the eleventh floor.

The desk was a slab of backlit onyx with no edge to grip. The woman behind it smiled with her whole trained face. "Checking in?"

"Delivery coordination for the foundation luncheon." Mira slid the printout across. Her voice came out flat and a little bored, the register of someone who did this at six buildings a week and had stopped being impressed by any of them. "Eleven."

The smile did not change while the woman's eyes went to the screen. This was the moment. Somewhere a server was deciding whether Mira's invented self survived contact, and the only thing Mira could control was the part of her that wanted to watch the screen too. She didn't. She let her eyes wander up the glass instead, toward the light, the bored upward drift of a person killing the seconds, and that was when the building stopped being abstract.

Through three stories of curtain wall, in a corner office that hung over the lobby like an aquarium, a silver-haired man stood with his hands clasped behind his back. Unhurried. He was speaking to someone seated, and the someone leaned forward to listen the way people lean when the person across from them owns the air. Mira knew the silver head from a hundred photographs and the calm even from this distance—it carried, that calm, it had a frequency. But it was the other man she felt in her teeth. Soft jaw, sandy hair going thin, a lanyard she couldn't read at this range and didn't need to. She had spent four nights with his face in Priya's files. *Logistics coordination, Riverside operations.* A name her sister had circled twice and never explained.

"Ms. Okafor?"

The catering name. Hers, today. Mira brought her eyes down, unhurried, the way you'd come back from any idle glance, and the cost of that unhurry was everything she had.

"Eleven, that's right." The woman printed a sticker, peeled it, slid it across the onyx. "Service elevators are around the corner past the café. You'll badge through with this for the day."

"Thanks." Mira lifted the sticker, pressed it to her jacket, and let her body turn her toward the café, away from the floating staircase and the man who held himself like a held breath.

She walked the long edge of the lobby, past the chemical lavender, past her own reflection sliding through forty feet of glass and refusing to slow down. The metallic smell got stronger near the elevator bank, and she understood now it was just the truth of the building leaking out from under its expensive perfume, the way the truth of a place always did if you kept your nose open and your face shut. Above her, behind the glass, the silver head was still talking. He had not looked down. He had no reason to. The whole tower had been built so that men like him would never have to.

Mira pressed the call button with one knuckle and kept her back, for as long as the open room allowed, to nothing at all.
