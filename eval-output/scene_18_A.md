# Scene 18 — Version A

**Prompt:** Write a darkly funny exchange between Mira and Daniel while they're patching up a wound, to relieve the tension.

**Word count:** 536

---

The peroxide hit the wound and fizzed white, and Mira hissed through her teeth like a kettle nobody had thought to take off the heat.

"Hold still," Daniel said.

"I am holding still."

"You're holding still the way a cat holds still before it ruins your day." He tilted the bottle again. The cut ran along the meat of her forearm, four inches, deep enough that the edges didn't want to sit together and shallow enough that she'd been able to drive home one-handed and lie about it for forty minutes. He'd smelled the blood on her jacket cuff before she'd gotten her shoes off. Old blood, metallic, the smell of a thing she'd been carrying around all evening.

"It's a scratch," she said.

"It's a scratch the way the Titanic took on water."

"That's not what a scratch is."

"No," Daniel agreed, threading the needle by the light over the bathroom sink. "It isn't." His thumb pressed the inside of her wrist, holding the arm flat against the porcelain, steadying the pulse he could feel jumping there. He worked the way he always had — slow, unhurried, building a little wall of competence between himself and the part of him that wanted to throw the needle across the room.

"I had it under control."

"You came home leaking. That's the opposite of control. That's the textbook definition of the thing control prevents."

"He had a knife. I didn't know he had a knife."

"That's worse." He set the first stitch. She didn't flinch, which was its own kind of confession. "You walked into a room where you didn't know what was in the room. That used to be the one thing you wouldn't do. You used to lecture me about it. You had a whole speech. There were bullet points."

"People grow."

"People grow. Cancer grows. Both technically growth." He tied off and started the second. "You want me to call this a scratch, fine. I'll write it down as a scratch. I'll tell anyone who asks. But I'm sitting here at midnight sewing my own personal disaster back together with thread from a hotel kit, and I'd just like it noted that the scratch and I have very different definitions of fine."

She watched him work. The scar through her eyebrow caught the light. "You don't have to be here."

"I know." He didn't look up. "That's the part that should worry you." Third stitch. Quieter. "I keep showing up for a woman who told me she'd bring me in and then went and got knifed in a room she didn't case. You want to talk about somebody who's lost control of his methods, we can do me next."

The fizzing had stopped. The wound just sat there now, neat, closed, undeniable.

"Priya would've cased the room," Mira said.

"Yeah." He cut the thread. "She would've." He didn't soften it. That was the thing he did instead of touching her face.

She looked at her reflection over his shoulder — pale, stitched, holding still at last — and reached for his hand where it rested on the sink, and stopped, fingers a half-inch short, and pulled her sleeve down over the whole conversation instead.
