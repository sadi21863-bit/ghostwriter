# Scene 08 — Version A

**Prompt:** Write the scene where Mira finally learns what actually happened to Priya the night she disappeared.

**Word count:** 1433

---

The man's name was Teodor Brandt, and he didn't sit until he'd checked the window twice.

Mira had picked the booth in the back of a diner that closed at midnight and opened again at five, which gave them the dead hour, the hour when the griddle was cold and the coffee tasted like the inside of the urn. She'd been awake for thirty-eight hours. She knew the number because she'd stopped trusting the way the fluorescent light hummed — it had started to pulse at the edges, a thing she could see only when she looked away from it.

"You don't have anything," Brandt said. He had a soft, careful voice, the voice of a man who'd spent decades not raising it. "I came because Reyes vouched for you, and because I wanted to see your face. But you don't have a warrant. You don't have a badge. You have a folder."

Her hands were flat on the table around the folder. She made them stay flat. There was a tremor in the right one that she could feel before she could see it, a fine vibration starting somewhere under the wrist, and she pressed the palm down harder until the table took it.

"I have what's in the folder," she said.

"Photographs of nothing. A timeline anyone could draw."

"I have you in three of them."

His chin lifted a half-inch. She watched the muscle along his jaw tighten — the masseter, bunching once and releasing — and she knew she'd put something into the room that he hadn't planned to find there. He looked at the folder the way you look at a dog you're not sure is chained.

"Tell me about the docks," she said. "The night my sister went dark."

And here was the thing she hadn't braced for, because she'd built her whole body around a different blow. She'd spent six days holding herself in the shape of a woman about to be told her sister was dead — sternum drawn in, breath kept shallow and high, the whole rib cage a fist clenched around the one fact she could survive only by knowing in advance. She'd practiced the grief the way you practice a fall.

Brandt rubbed his thumb along the rim of the coffee cup and said, "Kane didn't take her."

The words went in before she understood them. Her body moved first. Something released across the back of her shoulders, a sudden slackening she hadn't authorized, and her chin came up, her brow pulling inward and high — not the flat tension of someone braced, but the buckled-center furrow of someone who has been handed a thing that doesn't fit the hand she'd prepared.

"What," she said. Not a question. The word came out without the lift at the end. Her mouth had gone dry around it; she felt her tongue catch on the roof of her mouth.

"She ran," Brandt said. "Your sister saw our people positioning. She's smarter than the file says. She made us — made *me*, on the Tuesday — and by the time we moved Thursday she'd already burned her phone and gone." He turned the cup a quarter rotation. "Kane's been looking for her for six days. So have I. Same as you. You think you're chasing him. He's chasing her. He's terrified of what she'll do when she surfaces, because she had everything, Voss. Not pieces. Everything. She was going to walk it into a press room."

The fluorescent light pulsed. Mira blinked and it held still and then pulsed again, and she understood distantly that part of what was happening in the room was not in the room.

"You're lying." But her voice didn't have the iron she reached for. It came out thin. She heard it from a small distance, the way you hear yourself on a recording.

"Why would I." He spread one hand, weary. "You came here to make me afraid of you. I'm telling you the man I work for is afraid of a twenty-nine-year-old who walked out of her own apartment with a backpack. I'm telling you nobody buried her. She buried herself."

*She buried herself.*

Mira's hand came off the table. She didn't decide to lift it. It rose to her own sternum and pressed there, flat, the heel of the palm against the bone, the way you press a wound you can't see to find out if it's bleeding. Underneath the hand her heart was going wrong — not racing, the slow heavy strike of something that has lost the thread of its own rhythm. Her throat worked. She swallowed and there was nothing to swallow.

She had built the whole architecture of herself on a sentence. *Kane took my sister.* It was a load-bearing sentence. She had walked into rooms on it, lost sleep on it, decided who she was willing to become on it. And the man across the booth had pulled it out from under her in eleven words and now she was sitting inside a structure with no center, watching the light pulse, feeling her own pulse arrive late.

Priya had had everything. Priya had made the file. Priya had seen the danger and made a choice — the choice to vanish, to go silent, to let the world believe what it believed — and Mira had not been told. Mira had been *protected*, maybe. Or not considered. The two possibilities sat side by side and she couldn't make her exhausted mind hold them apart.

"Where," she got out. The word cracked in the middle. She tried again. "If she ran — if she's — where would she—"

"I don't know." Brandt watched her with something that wasn't quite pity; pity would have been cleaner. It was the careful attention of a man watching to see how badly the news had landed, because how badly it landed told him things. "And before you decide your next move — think about what surfacing her does. You drag her into the light, you do Kane's work for him. He can't find her. You find her, you've found her *for* him." He stood. He buttoned his coat with the deliberation of the cold, though the diner wasn't cold. "Your sister chose the dark, Voss. The question you have to answer is whether you can stand to leave her in it."

He went. The bell over the door made a sound she didn't hear so much as feel, a small disturbance in the air.

Mira stayed in the booth.

The tremor in her right hand had spread, or she'd stopped fighting it; it ran up into the forearm now, a low insistent shake, and she watched it with a strange detachment, this hand that wouldn't stop, this hand that had been so steady on the table. The grief she'd packed her body around for six days had nowhere to discharge. There was no death to grieve. There was only a door her sister had closed from the inside, with Mira on the wrong side of it, and the terrible new arithmetic that her sister might have closed it *for* her.

She thought of Daniel — *I'll bring you in, I promise, the second I have something* — and the promise sat in her chest like a stone she'd swallowed and forgotten. She had not brought him in. She had come here alone, on no sleep, and gotten the one piece of information that changed everything, and now she didn't even have someone across the table to say it out loud to, to make it real, to tell her whether what she was feeling was relief that Priya might be alive or shame that she'd needed her sister to be a victim to know who she was.

The coffee had gone cold. She hadn't touched it. She pressed her palm harder against her sternum and breathed, once, all the way down, and the breath came out shaking, and she let it, because there was no one in the dead-hour diner to see her do it, and because some part of her — the analyst, the part that never stopped — had already understood that the simple story was gone now, and would not be coming back, and that everything she did from here would have two faces and she would have to choose, alone, which one she could live behind.

She did not yet have a word for the feeling.

She just sat with her hand on her chest, in the pulsing light, and let her body know it first.
