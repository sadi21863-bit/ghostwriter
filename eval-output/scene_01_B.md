# Scene 01 — Version B

**Prompt:** Continue the story: Mira reads Daniel's message about the docks and has to decide whether to go alone.

**Word count:** 960

The laundromat was still running its last cycle of the night. Three machines, none of them belonging to anyone Mira could see. The fluorescent tube above the change machine had been flickering for six weeks, and she had never reported it to anyone, because it was the kind of detail that made a place forgettable.

She reached into the coin return without looking. The paper was there. Folded twice, thin as a receipt.

She took it to the row of plastic chairs nearest the dryers and sat. The heat from the machines came off in waves, that particular institutional warmth that smelled of someone else's clothes, of detergent that was trying too hard. She unfolded the message with both hands flat on her thighs.

*Heard something about your old beat. Hardware moving. Tonight.*

No signature. No question mark after it. Daniel didn't ask questions in writing.

She read it twice more, the paper held level, her eyes not moving past the last word. Then she looked at the coin return from across the room.

Their old dead drop had been a café in the Meridian district, a napkin dispenser in the third booth from the back. She hadn't been there in two years. Nobody had, because nobody was supposed to remember it. But the laundromat was hers — the address she'd never given him officially, never spoken aloud in any building with a badge reader on the door. He'd found it the way Daniel found most things: by watching her long enough to know where she'd go when she stopped trusting rooms.

Which meant he'd been here. Inside the perimeter she'd thought was clean. He'd put the message in the coin return — unsigned, untraceable, written in language that would mean nothing to anyone who didn't already know — and he'd walked back out.

He wasn't offering to come. He was making sure she couldn't ask him to.

She folded the message along its original creases and pressed it flat against her palm. The paper was room temperature now, whatever warmth it had carried from his hands already gone. Outside the laundromat's single window, the street was dark in the way that city streets are never fully dark, everything stained the color of old amber by the lights she couldn't see the source of. She pulled out her phone. Opened the map. The docks resolved on the screen in satellite grey, the river a black seam to the east, the loading yards marked by nothing useful — no pins, no notes, just the shape of the place and the sodium-orange scatter of the streetlamps along the access road, still burning in the image like something that had never been turned off.

Hardware moving. Tonight.

She thought about what hardware meant in the context of a foundation that laundered surveillance infrastructure through nonprofit grants, in the context of a man whose panic at a confirmation hearing had the particular pitch of someone who knew exactly which floor of the building was on fire. Not servers. Not files. Not the kind of hardware you moved in a truck without people who'd been told the freight didn't exist.

Priya had said *the docks* in her last message. Six words, three days before she went dark.

Mira put the map away. She sat for another moment with the dryers pushing warm air against the side of her face, listening to the tumbling of someone's laundry — a rhythmic, ordinary sound that had no connection to any of this, and so felt, right now, almost unbearable in its steadiness.

She could call him. The number would connect. He'd answer the way he always answered, with his name and nothing else, and she would hear in the half-second before he spoke whether she was waking him or whether he'd been sitting in his own version of this — a dark room, a phone face-down, waiting to see if she was going to make the choice that meant he'd have to decide about his.

She picked up the phone. His name was in her contacts under a word she'd never told anyone the meaning of. Her thumb was a centimeter above the screen.

The dryer closest to her shuddered through its final cycle and went silent. The one beside it followed, and then the third, and the room was abruptly very quiet, just the flicker of the bad tube overhead and the distant friction of traffic on wet pavement. In the silence she could hear herself thinking, which was worse.

He'd left the message unsigned. He'd come to her building, her laundromat, her coin return — and left it unsigned. That was the answer. Not to the question she was about to ask, but to every question she'd been building toward since the moment she'd understood that loyalty had a different architecture than she'd assumed. Daniel hadn't chosen Kane. He hadn't chosen against her. He had chosen the narrow, survivable path that kept him able to move in the world she'd been removed from, and he had given her what he could from that position, and it was not nothing.

It was also not enough to bring into a dark shipping yard where someone might already be waiting to make sure the freight stayed quiet.

She set the phone face-down on her thigh. Looked at it. Looked at the coin return, still empty, its little mouth open in the wall.

Then she stood, pocketed the message, and crossed to the exit. The door had a long horizontal push bar, cold and bar-metal blunt. She pressed both palms against it and felt it give.

She stopped.

Behind her, the fluorescent tube flickered once, twice, and held.

Her hand was still on her phone. She hadn't put it away.
