# Scene 10 — Version A

**Prompt:** Write the scene where Mira is trapped in the flooded sub-basement of her safehouse and realizes she isn't alone down there.

**Word count:** 568

---

The water came up to her shins, and that was normal. The garment district sat below the river and always had; the safehouse had been flooded the day she rented it and would be flooded the day she left. Mira knew the depth by the cold line it drew on her calves. She knew the way the single bulb over the workbench swung when the laundromat's dryers ran upstairs, throwing the room into a slow tilt of shadow and back. She knew the smell — mildew and gun oil, the river's iron underneath. She had memorized this place the way she memorized everything now, because the things she didn't memorize were the things that got taken from her.

She was down here for the drive. Taped under the third joist from the stairs, wrapped in a freezer bag, the only copy of the dock manifests with Kane's foundation in the routing. She waded toward it with one hand out for the joist, the bulb swinging, her shadow stretching long across the far wall and shrinking back.

The water moved against her shins. A small wave, lapping forward.

She stopped.

The dryers were not running. The bulb hung still. Nothing upstairs was making the floor shudder, and she had not moved fast enough to push a wave that size, and the water came forward again and touched the backs of her knees, a second pulse, patient, the way water moves when something large has shifted in it somewhere behind you.

Her mouth went dry. Not gradually. All at once, like a tap closing. She did not turn around. The part of her that had kept her alive through three countries had her hand already on the grip at the small of her back, and the part of her that was still a person stood very still and listened past the ringing that had risen in her ears, listened for breathing, and heard her own, and heard, under it, slower, a second draw of air with a wet catch at the bottom of it.

She turned.

The bulb chose that moment to be still, and so the light did not swing, and what stood at the foot of the stairs simply stood there in it. Sharper-dressed than Mira, always had been, the blazer dark with water to the collar. Used to being underestimated. Priya was looking at her with her head tilted just slightly past where a neck wants to go, water sheeting off the hem of her sleeve in a thin unbroken ribbon that did not stop, did not drip, ran and ran as though it had been running for a long time, and her sister's eyes were open and fixed on Mira's face with the flat attention of the evil-eyed, and her mouth was open too, and from it came that wet, patient, second breath.

She did not move. Mira's nervous system screamed at her to read the scene and the scene would not resolve — the dry-cleaned shoulders, the river soaked into the hair she had braided for her sister a hundred times, the standing in nine inches of water with the trouser cuffs floating up around the ankles like something already let go of the bottom.

"Priya," she said, and her voice came out of her wrong, too high, a child's.

The water touched the backs of her knees again.

Priya had not taken a step.
