# Scene 03 — Version A

**Prompt:** Write a quiet conversation between Mira and Daniel where he admits he's been talking to Kane's office behind her back.

**Word count:** 1810

---

The parking garage was four floors of poured concrete holding onto the day's cold like it meant something. Mira heard his truck before she saw it — the specific knock of a diesel engine that Daniel had been meaning to fix for three years. She'd counted the months by it. She stood one level below the exit ramp, where the fluorescent strip above her had given up entirely, and she watched him pull into the space opposite without signaling. Old habit. Agency habit. Don't announce.

He took a moment in the cab before he got out. She gave him that.

When he did, his jacket was wrong for the weather — too light, the collar turned up against a wind that wasn't reaching this far down — and she recognized it as the jacket he wore when he wanted to look like a man who had somewhere else to be.

"You drove forty minutes," she said.

"Forty-two." He stopped at the rear quarter of the truck, one hand in his pocket. "Traffic on the bridge."

The AU4 was already there, the brow pulled in and down, the bilateral crease she associated with him working something through and not liking where it was landing. She'd seen that face across field assessments, across deposition rooms, across a hospital corridor once that neither of them talked about. He wasn't angry. He was braced.

Her chest registered it before she gave it a name. A tightening, low and lateral, the kind that preceded the thing you'd been trying not to know.

"There's a number," she said. "In your recent calls. K-area code. You called it four times in six days."

He didn't look away. That, at least.

"You went through my phone."

"I went through your cloud backup." She paused. "You should change your recovery password. Your dog's name is obvious."

Something crossed his face that wasn't quite a wince. The chin came up a fraction — AU17, the muscle under the lip, the thing that appeared when people were keeping something in through sheer jaw pressure — and then he released it deliberately, the way a man puts down something heavy in a controlled way so he can say he set it down rather than dropped it.

"Mira."

"Don't do the name thing. That's what you do when you want me to slow down."

"It's what I do," he said, "when I want you to hear me."

The metallic smell of the truck's interior bled into the cold air between them — oil, the particular synthetic warmth of a heater that had been running, something human and closed-in beneath it. She was aware of her own hands. The right one had risen to the door frame of the truck without her directing it, fingers loose, and she registered the impulse to drum them once and stopped herself. Stopped the stopping. Let her hand fall.

"Who picked up?" she said.

He shifted his weight. Not away from her — that was important, she noted it — but a settling, the way a man plants his feet when he's decided to stand in a thing rather than sidestep it.

"A woman named Hartley. She's not on any org chart I can find, which means she's either legal or she's operations and they've been careful about it."

"What did she want."

It wasn't a question, quite. The flatness of it was intentional, and he heard the flatness, and she watched him decide whether to respond to what she'd said or what she'd meant.

"She said they were open to a conversation," he said. "Career restoration. Some kind of immunity framework. She used the phrase 'mutual accommodation,' which I've been turning over ever since because it's the kind of phrase designed to sound like both people benefit when only one of them actually will."

"And you went back three more times."

"I went back," he said, and there was something in his voice — the pitch dropping slightly, the rate slowing in a way that wasn't hesitation but was close to it — "because the second call, she asked me something specific."

He stopped there. A breath. The cold was genuine, the kind that found the gap between collar and skin, and his exhale was briefly visible.

Her body knew. The tightening in her chest went sharper and lateral, the somatic signal arriving fully formed before she'd constructed the thought around it. She waited for the thought.

"She asked if you knew who leaked the files." His eyes were on her. Not shifting, not managing — just there. "Not what you knew. Who."

The fluorescent strip above her buzzed once and held. Somewhere above them, a car moved across the top level, the tire sound dampened by distance into something almost oceanic.

She heard herself say, very quietly, "They don't care about the leak."

"No."

"The leak is over. It's out. Whatever was going to happen from the leak has already happened." She was working through it aloud and she knew it and she let herself, because Daniel was the only person she trusted to follow the reasoning and tell her where she went wrong. "They're hunting the person. Because the person knows something the leak didn't contain."

He nodded once. Slow.

"Kane's people think you're close to it," he said. "Whatever it is. They think you've been working something. They're not wrong."

"You told them that."

"I told them I hadn't spoken to you in three weeks." His jaw was set. "Which was true at the time."

The gap between what he'd said and what she needed to do with it opened up in front of her like a drop in the floor she'd been about to walk across. Kane wasn't managing a leak. Kane was managing a person who could testify to something. Someone who knew what had actually happened — at the docks, before the docks, in whatever sequence of events ended with Priya's phone going dark.

And Kane thought Mira was close to that person.

Which meant Kane thought the real leaker was still reachable.

Which meant — and here the somatic marker hit her, cold and definitive, like putting a hand against something you already knew would be frozen — Priya might be that person.

Might still be.

She became aware that she'd stopped breathing in a normal way. She corrected it. Daniel was watching her make the correction.

"You could have called me," she said.

"I was going to."

"You were managing me." Her voice came out level, which was something she controlled deliberately — the pitch held, the prosody stripped, because if she let the prosody move she didn't know what would come out. "You've been negotiating with his people, and you were going to tell me when you'd gotten far enough that I couldn't do anything about it. You were going to present me with the version where it was already handled."

He didn't deny it. That was Daniel — he wouldn't insult her with the denial.

"I thought if I could get you immunity," he said, "before whatever you're walking toward—"

"You don't get to decide what I walk toward."

"No," he said. "But I get to decide what I'm willing to watch."

The sentence sat between them and neither of them touched it. Above, another car. The buzz of the one functioning strip somewhere deeper in the structure. His hands were out of his pockets now, and she could see that they were not entirely steady — a faint tremor that he'd always had when his sympathetic system was running hot, a thing she'd noticed in their first year working together and never mentioned, because some things you learn about a person and you simply carry.

"Everything I have," she said, "points to Kane's panic being disproportionate. Bigger than career preservation. It's been bigger from the start, and I couldn't work out what was underneath it until—" She stopped. Made a choice. "If Priya knew what the leak didn't contain, and she was going to come forward, and then she went to the docks—"

"Don't," he said. Low. The word doing something more than its definition.

"I'm already there. You know I'm already there."

His face changed then. The sustained AU4 released and what replaced it was something that had no easy anatomical label — the AU6 wanting to appear, the genuine Duchenne signal, the thing that happens in the upper cheek when a person is feeling something they haven't defended against yet, but held back by the compressed set of the mouth, the AU17 doing its work, the whole face caught between the thing it wanted to do and the thing he was making it do instead.

He looked, for a moment, like a man who had been trying very hard to protect something and had just understood it wasn't his to protect.

"What do you need from me," he said.

Not *what do you want*. Daniel always knew the difference.

She looked at him. The cold was in her hands — she'd been holding them at her sides and they'd gone to the particular numbness that started at the fingertips and moved inward. She didn't move to warm them.

"Tell me everything Hartley asked. In sequence. Every word you can reconstruct." She paused. "And then tell me what you told her."

He held her gaze. One beat, two.

"That's going to take a while."

"I know."

He reached back and opened the truck's door, the dome light coming on in the interior — that trapped warmth again, the smell of him and his dog and the long drives he took when he couldn't sleep. He didn't get in. He was leaving the decision to her.

She moved to the passenger side. She got in. She didn't look at him while she closed the door and the dome light went out and they were in the particular dark of a parking garage at night, which was not quite darkness but its own thing, structural and cold and without ambiguity.

He started the engine. Let it run.

"Mira."

She waited.

"If I walk away from this," he said. "Right now. Just — get out and drive." He wasn't looking at her. His hands were on the wheel and his eyes were on the concrete wall in front of the truck. His voice was quiet in the way that some things are quiet because they have weight. "Will you hunt me the way you're hunting him?"

The heater was beginning to push warm air into the cab. She could hear his breathing. She could hear her own. The diesel knock was still there under everything, the thing that needed fixing, the thing that hadn't been fixed in three years.

She didn't answer.

The question stayed in the truck with them, alive and specific, and she let it stay.
