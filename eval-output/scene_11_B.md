# Scene 11 — Version B

**Prompt:** Write the scene where Mira finally decodes the shell-company list and realizes it connects directly to Kane's confirmation hearing.

**Word count:** 1030

---

The decryption had been running for eleven hours, and Mira had counted most of them.

She sat with her back against the damp brick, laptop balanced on a milk crate, watching the progress bar that wasn't really a bar at all—just a counter, white digits cycling through key combinations faster than the eye could resolve. Two point seven billion possibilities and the machine chewed through them with a fan-whine that had become the sound of the room. The basement smelled of mildew and overheated silicon and the burnt-bottom coffee neither of them had finished. Water beaded on the pipe overhead and dropped, every ninety seconds or so, into a saucepan she'd set on the floor three days ago.

Daniel stood by the stairs. He always stood by the stairs, between her and the only door, the way he stood in every room, like a man who had decided the exits were his job. He'd taken his jacket off an hour ago. The shoulder holster made him look heavier than he was.

"Call it in," he said. Not the first time. "You've got enough already. The bank routing, the Foundation's name on three of the four shells. Give it to Internal Review and let the institution eat itself."

"The institution is the thing that ate me, Daniel." She didn't look up. "I hand this to Internal Review, it goes to a committee, the committee goes to a recess, and Kane sits his hearing in seventy hours with a smile and a glass of water."

"You don't know that."

"I know exactly that." She did look up then, and made herself soften it, because his face was tired in the way that meant he was about to apologize for caring. "Three layers. I thought it was one. It's three. The last node's nested inside the second, inside the first, like someone built it for a person who'd get curious and quit. I'm not quitting."

He crossed the room, and the floor gave its wet exhale under his weight. He set a fresh cup down on the crate beside her, the saucepan caught its drip, and he stayed crouched there a moment longer than the coffee required.

"You said you'd loop me in," he said. "The whole way. You said that in this room."

"You're in this room."

"That's not the same and you know it." His jaw worked. He was a man who chose his words and then resented having to. "I lost a year of my life cleaning up after your last solo run. I'm not afraid of you being wrong, Mira. I'm afraid of you being right and going there alone."

The counter stopped.

It didn't beep. There was no triumphant chime—she'd never built one in, contempt for the dramatics of it. The fan-whine simply dropped an octave as the load fell off the processor, and a directory unspooled itself down the screen in plain monospaced text. Shell names. Dates. A waterfall of them.

She read the first column and her stomach didn't lurch the way she'd expected. It went cold and very still, which was worse.

"It's institutional," she said. "It's not a slush fund. It's a procurement chain. Look—the Foundation's clean public account routes to the first shell, the first shell to a defense subcontractor, the subcontractor back to a grant the Foundation publicly *received*. It's a loop. He's been funding the surveillance build with money he laundered through his own charity and called philanthropy at the ribbon-cuttings."

Daniel leaned over her shoulder. "That's the hearing. That program's the whole pitch of his hearing. National Security Oversight—he's supposed to be the man who *reins it in*."

"He built it." She scrolled. "He built the thing he's promising to leash."

And then she stopped scrolling, because the dates kept going up the screen, and up, past the confirmation announcement, past the Foundation's incorporation, and she sat there doing arithmetic she didn't want the answer to.

The first transaction was timestamped eleven years ago. The Foundation was three. The hearing was a rumor that didn't exist yet. Kane's whole public face—the oversight crusader, the reformed insider, the only adult in the room—was eight years younger than the operation he was supposedly going to govern.

"This was never about the career," she said quietly. "You don't protect a career you don't have yet. He didn't build this to climb. The climb was so he'd be standing on top of it when it mattered." She heard her own voice flatten into the analyst's register, the one that had survived everything else about her. "The leak didn't threaten his promotion. It threatened the thing the promotion was *for*. That's the disproportion. That's why he burned me without blinking. I was just whoever happened to be near the wound."

Daniel said nothing. His breathing had changed—she could hear it now, slow and deliberate, the breathing of a man holding still on purpose.

Her secure phone buzzed against the crate.

One short pulse. Not a call. A message, on the line that had no number, the line three people in the world knew and one of them was her, and she'd checked the routing herself and it was supposed to be untraceable in both directions.

She picked it up.

An image loaded, line by line, the way images loaded in this basement where the signal had to bounce off something to reach her. A wristwatch, cheap, with a face cracked across the six and the seven—the crack she'd made herself, age twelve, slamming a car door on her sister's wrist, the crack Priya had never gotten fixed because *it's a good story, Mira, it's the only story where you lost*. The watch lay flat on a folded newspaper. The dateline was visible above the fold. Yesterday's.

The drip hit the saucepan.

Mira looked at the photograph. She looked at the directory still glowing on the monitor, eleven years of proof, the thing that could postpone the hearing, end the man, vindicate every burned year of her life. She looked at the cracked watch face, the date she could read without zooming.

Daniel's voice came from very close. "Mira. What is it."

She didn't answer him.
