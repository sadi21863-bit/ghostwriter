# Scene 05 — Version A

**Prompt:** Write the scene where Mira fights off two of Kane's security contractors at the Riverside Docks.

**Word count:** 949

---

The fog tastes of rust and brine, and Mira is breathing it too fast. She knows she's breathing too fast. The first contractor came around the container stack on her left and the second is somewhere behind the forklift, and she has maybe four seconds before they decide she's worth more dead than questioned.

The first one closes the distance. He's big, moves like he's done this — weight low, hands open, no knife yet. Good. A knife now would be stupid this close and he knows it.

He reaches for her jacket. She doesn't pull back, she steps in, which is the only thing that works against a grip, and drives the heel of her right hand up under his chin. Not a fist. Knuckles break on jaws. The heel catches him on the maxilla and his head snaps back maybe three inches, enough. Her left hand is already on his wrist, peeling the grab off her collar, and she folds his fingers the wrong way until something in the joint gives with a wet click she feels more than hears.

He grunts. Doesn't fold. They never fold the way you want.

His free hand comes around in a hook and she eats part of it on the shoulder, ducking, and the impact spins her half a step into the wet steel of the container. Cold through her jacket. Her ear rings on that side. Sound on the left goes thin and far away, the docks reduced to her own pulse and the slap of water against the hull behind her.

She doesn't think about the second man. She can't. Tunnel.

The big one comes again and now he's angry, telegraphing, dropping his good hand to grab. She lets him commit, pivots off her lead foot, and his momentum carries past her hip. She hooks his trailing ankle with her instep and shoves at the back of his shoulder. Physics does the rest. He goes down hard onto the concrete, palms slapping, the broken fingers taking weight they shouldn't and the sound he makes this time is higher.

She should get on him. She knows that. The ground is where this ends. But the second man.

The second man is here.

She catches him in her ruined left peripheral as a shape, not a person, and turns into it a half-beat slow. His forearm catches her across the throat — not a strike, a clothesline, all his body weight behind it — and the world tilts. She's down. Concrete punches the air out of her, and that's worse than the throat, the breath gone, the long terrible second where the lungs won't fill.

He's on her before she can roll. Knee in her sternum, hands at her collar, and she can smell cigarettes and cold sweat. Her vision narrows to a gray ring. She gets a thumb into the soft place under his jaw, the angle of the mandible, presses up into the nerve and he jerks his head away from the pressure, which is what she needs — his weight shifts off her chest by an inch and she gets one knee up between them, then the other, frames her forearms across his chest, and bridges.

He doesn't come all the way off. But he's not settled anymore, and that's enough to breathe — one ragged half-lungful, fire down the windpipe where the clothesline landed.

Behind her the big one is getting up. She can hear him swearing through the thin gray distance, getting his feet under him.

Two of them and she has seconds, not minutes. Nobody fights two trained men for minutes. Her arms are already shaking against the second man's weight, the bridge collapsing.

So she stops bridging. Drops flat, pulls him forward into the empty space, and as he falls toward her she turns her head and drives the point of her elbow up into his temple as he comes. The angle is bad. It half-lands, scrapes off the brow, but his eyes do the thing eyes do — a flutter, a quarter-second where the lights brown out — and in that quarter-second she shrimps her hips out from under him and gets her back off the ground.

The big one's hand closes on her hair.

She turns into the pull instead of away, because turning away tears scalp and turning in just hurts, and her hand finds something on the ground — a length of strapping, a wrench, she doesn't look, fingers too cold to feel what it is exactly, only that it's hard and it's a foot long. She swings it backhand into the side of his knee.

He drops to that side with a bark. The hand leaves her hair.

She's up. She doesn't know how, the knees protesting, the throat a single bar of fire, the left ear still dead and ringing. Two men on the ground, one with a knee, one half-conscious, and she is so tired already, thirty seconds in, her arms gone to water.

She runs.

Not toward the office where Priya's name might be in a manifest. The other way, into the fog, because she is alive and she will not be in ten seconds if she stays, and Daniel was right, Daniel is always right, *you go in there alone you don't come out* — and she hates that she can hear his voice over her own ragged breathing, hates that she needed it.

Behind her, the big one is shouting into a radio. The fog swallows the words. She runs until the sodium lights blur and the wrench is still in her cold fist and she cannot make her fingers let it go.
